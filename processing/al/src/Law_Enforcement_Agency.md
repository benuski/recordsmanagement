## Historical Context {#historical-context .H2}

Alongside state militia units, local law enforcement entities such as
county sheriffs, municipal police, and constabularies satisfied
Alabama's law enforcement needs for the first century of statehood. By
the 1920s, new challenges including Prohibition and widespread adoption
of the automobile strained the capacity of these traditional policing
institutions. Policing the rapidly changing twentieth-century social
landscape meant enshrining civil law enforcement powers at the state
level of government. The following provides a brief historical narrative
of state policing in Alabama, centering on the converging histories of
the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency's (ALEA) two major policing houses --
the Department of Public Safety and the State Bureau of Investigations. 

In January 1919, Alabama legislators anticipated the federal Volstead
Act by passing a state-level Prohibition (Alabama Act 1919-007) on
"spirituous, vinous, malted, fermented, or other intoxicating or
prohibited liquors and beverages." To aid its enforcement, this act
recognized two new expressions of gubernatorial powers. The first
appointed the governor as "the executive head of the law enforcement
machinery of the State \[who is\] authorized to supervise, direct and
give orders to any and all officers of the State or of any county in the
State," specifically with respect to enforcing Prohibition-related laws.
The second conferred upon the governor the separate power "to appoint
and employ inspectors or secret service men, or such other persons as he
may deem necessary for the purpose of detecting and reporting upon
violations of said \[Prohibition\] laws, or any \[laws\]."

During the same session, legislators approved Alabama Act 1919-551,
which further codified the governor's prerogatives to "employ a special
force of not more than ten men to serve under his immediate direction
and control \[which force of men\] may perform their functions and
pursue their activities anywhere and at all times through the State,
irrespective of local political subdivisions of the State, and shall see
that the laws of the State are enforced, and may make arrests in all
cases and under such conditions as arrests are made by the sheriffs and
other officials under the laws of the State." Together, these acts
placed all of Alabama's existing law officers under the governor's
direction for Prohibition-related enforcement while also granting the
governor the prerogative to outfit up to ten men with state-wide
policing authority. This ten-man outfit became the State Law Enforcement
Department (SLED), one of Alabama's earliest regular state police
forces.[^1] While SLED officers primarily enforced federal and state
Prohibition laws, they also investigated murders, robberies, arson,
forgeries, kidnappings, and various misdemeanors.[^2]

SLED's small ten-man force mostly served in auxiliary roles alongside
existing local law enforcement (mostly sheriffs) and federal forces
(mostly Prohibition Agents from the Revenue Department) already
operating in Alabama. Through these partnerships, the understaffed SLED
was able to flex gubernatorial authority into arenas where extant police
powers were compromised or insufficient. SLED's success, despite its
modest size, was so notable that its annual budget increased fivefold to
\$100,000.00 by 1924, and by 1927, Governor Brandon was urging the
Legislature to increase SLED's force by twenty additional officers. In a
corollary to his suggested addition of twenty new officers, Governor
Brandon also advocated that the enlarged force be used "for the
enforcement of the traffic laws on the highways of the State, viz:
reckless driving, persons driving or riding on the highways of the State
while intoxicated, and the overloading of vehicles, etc.," and that half
of the twenty new officers be deployed exclusively as "motorcycle scouts
in the highways."[^3] Additionally, Governor Brandon's speech presented
fresh policies on the SLED's funding sources and operational purview,
specifically that SLED's funding originate from fines issued by the
department rather than from the state's General Fund.

While Governor Bibb Graves later echoed Governor Brandon's praise for
SLED (Graves being particularly pleased by how the department's fines
and licensing operations bolstered state coffers), gubernatorial
patronage for SLED soon broke upon the rock of Governor Miller.[^4]
Indeed, soon after assuming office in 1931, Governor Miller mothballed
SLED amid the strains of the Great Depression, having characterized the
force as "an extravagance and a source of constant annoyance and
irritation" as well as simply "unnecessary."[^5] However, Governor
Miller ensured that "the Statutes permitting \[SLED officers'\]
appointment and making appropriations for their compensation should not
be repealed; so they can become operative any time, if my opinion should
prove wrong."[^6]

Despite its brief existence, the SLED experiment provided Alabama
lawmakers valuable experience designing and deploying a standing state
police power. SLED's ranks also produced influential leaders like Chief
Law Enforcement Officer Walter McAdory, who would later head the Alabama
Department of Public Safety (DPS). While at DPS, McAdory also assigned
some of his DPS officers to a "special investigators" corps, creating
the precursor to Alabama's State Bureau of Investigations.[^7]

***Department of Public Safety (DPS)***

Alongside Prohibition enforcement, state policing power needed to evolve
to face the new challenges of automobiles. As evidenced in Governor
Brandon's 1927 speech to the Legislature, some leaders had begun
conceptualizing state highway patrol as an essential duty of a state
police force. One natural choice to patrol highways was the same state
power responsible for building those roadways -- the State Highway
Department.[^8]

Alabama legislators created the State Highway Department (SHD) in 1911,
charging the department with the planning, construction, and maintenance
of "all public roads, culverts and bridges of the State."[^9] Efforts of
the SHD soon saw new highways crisscrossing the state. Yet, since these
new state highways intersected many local law enforcement jurisdictions,
patrol and enforcement responsibilities on these roadways were unclear.
While keeping peace around a town square was the straightforward task of
a police officer or sheriff's deputy, providing holistic coverage for
state highways was beyond local law enforcement's jurisdictional and
operational scopes. Moreover, since the extant but understaffed State
Law Enforcement Department primarily focused on Prohibition-related
crimes, the patrol of these sprawling veins of commerce fell to the
State Highway Department.

Thus, in 1927, SHD received enforcement prerogatives over state roadway
violations as part of its highway-related directives. SHD's early law
enforcement activities included licensing automobile drivers and
ticketing roadway violations. SHD's limited jurisdictional authority
meant that its officers were only empowered "to make arrests for the
violation...of the road laws of the State of Alabama."[^10] Despite such
a narrow enforcement directive, SHD soon faced the same reality that the
State Law Enforcement Department had, namely that SHD's, "patrol of ten
men was...entirely inadequate to handle the enforcement of the \[motor
carrier laws, motor vehicle license laws, and gasoline tax laws\]
throughout the state."[^11]

The lesson was clear: Despite their successes, the auxiliary-oriented
designs of both SHD's Highway Patrol and the State Law Enforcement
Department were insufficient to sustain an effective state police force.
A more focused, standalone state police power was needed.

An opportunity presented itself in 1935, when the people of Alabama
reelected Bibb Graves to the governorship. In his opening message to the
Legislature that year, Governor Graves reiterated his earlier 1931
support for vehicle licensing and associated state law enforcement
measures. With Governor Grave's backing, the Legislature passed Alabama
Act 1935-331, creating a new standing state law enforcement entity --
the State Highway Patrol (SHP).

Nonetheless, the transition from an auxiliary to a standing state police
paradigm was neither clear-cut nor immediate, as evidenced by the fact
that the Legislature continued to fund policing responsibilities for the
State Highway Department (via Alabama Act 1935-19) while simultaneously
passing Alabama Act 1935-331 to create the State Highway Patrol. The
State Highway Commission's 1937 Annual Report highlighted the complex
relationship between the two bodies, declaring "\[t\]he Alabama State
Highway Patrol, although not under the direct supervision of the Alabama
State Highway Commission, functions in close cooperation with the State
Highway Department."[^12] This cooperation was so close that the 1937
annual report by the State Highway Department subsumed the inaugural
annual report of the State Highway Patrol.[^13]

Ultimately, while tasked with similar-sounding duties, the
legislatively-funded State Highway Commission's policing authority was
limited to road law violations, while the State Highway Patrol -- funded
solely by new motor vehicle licensing fees, fines, and forfeitures --
fielded officers with a broader jurisdiction.[^14] The State Highway
Patrol's model was an immediate success. Where the State Highway
Department's patrol had become bogged down amid the department's
manifold obligations, the State Highway Patrol paradigm centered on a
clear and robust law enforcement mandate.

In 1939, building upon lessons learned from SLED and SHD's enforcement
operations and the State Highway Patrol's resounding success, the
Legislature approved the creation of the Alabama Department of Public
Safety (DPS). This even larger administrative apparatus for state
policing subsumed the State Highway Patrol and also introduced new
divisions entitled the Driver's License Division and the Identification
and Investigation Division (the latter of which would evolve into the
State Bureau of Investigations).[^15] In addition to inheriting the
State Highway Patrol's funding source of drivers' licensing fees and
fines, the DPS also secured an appropriation from the state's General
Fund staring in 1955. This dual-source funding granted the department
increased financial security.[^16]

The Department of Public Safety is today organized into four divisions
and fields most of the agency's uniformed agents. The Highway Patrol
Division covers canine deployment, traffic homicides, motor carrier
safety and enforcement, and communications personnel related to state
roadways. On state waterways, Marine Patrol Division troopers perform
similar enforcement tasks as their highway colleagues. The Law
Enforcement Training Center constitutes the third division, providing
entry and continuing education for state troopers and other law
enforcement in the state. Lastly, the Driver License Division supports
eight distinct units covering every aspect of the licensing application,
testing, reinstatement, and hearings processes.

***State Bureau of Investigations (SBI)***

Within weeks of the State Highway Patrol's creation in 1935, the need to
distinguish patrol responsibilities from investigative tasks resulted in
the reclassification of officers based on patrol or investigation roles.
Building on his experience overseeing the earlier "State Law Enforcement
Department," SHP Chief Walter McAdory selected two patrolmen from the
seventy-four-man force to become "special investigators."[^17] By 1947,
the efforts of McAdory's two special investigators had grown to
constitute the Investigative and Identification (I&I) Unit within DPS's
Highway Patrol Division.[^18] Between 1947 and 1974, the constitution
and nomenclature of the DPS unit housing "special investigators" changed
often, owing to evolving interpretations of the role and utility of
special investigators. In 1974, Governor Wallace christened the Alabama
Bureau of Investigation as the administrative home of the DPS
investigators, and, in time, the bureau constituted its own distinct
division within DPS.[^19] Later, pursuant to the creation of ALEA, the
Alabama Bureau of Investigation was administratively separated from DPS,
provisioned with its own command structure, and renamed the "State
Bureau of Investigations."[^20]

ALEA's State Bureau of Investigations has a trifurcated structure -- the
Criminal Investigation Division, whose agents generate investigative
data in the performance of their field operations; the Criminal Justice
Information Services Division, whose data experts consolidate, manage,
and make data available; and the Alabama Fusion Center, whose analysts
use data to generate and disseminate actionable intelligence. Most SBI
investigative tasks are performed in collaboration with local or federal
law enforcement, such as by providing advanced investigative resources
to local jurisdictions or contributing intelligence to federal
authorities for interstate operations.

The Criminal Investigation Division commands the majority of the State's
investigative agents and assets, including the Unmanned Aerial Systems
unit, the state bomb squad, the state SWAT Team, the Special Victims
Unit, the Vice and Special Operations Squads, the Major Crimes Unit, and
the Special Projects Office.

SBI also houses the Alabama Criminal Justice Information Services
Division (CJIS), which is responsible for "providing and maintaining
mission-critical criminal justice information systems and services," in
the State of Alabama.[^21] Staff offer training, provide guidance, and
monitor compliance with the criminal justice information requirements
outlined by the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the Alabama Justice
Information Commission.[^22] Within the Division, staff are organized
into the Automated Biometric Identification System (ABIS), Criminal
History Repository, Criminal Justice Compliance, Sex Offender Registry,
and Uniform Crime Reporting Program Sections.[^23]

Finally, the Fusion Center promotes information sharing between federal,
state, local, tribal, and territorial agencies and serves as a
clearinghouse for intelligence related to crime prevention and
counterterrorism. The Fusion Center does not investigate criminal
activity. Instead, they support investigations by providing intelligence
to other investigative agencies. Alabama Fusion Center analysts' areas
of expertise include cold cases, cybersecurity intelligence, human
trafficking, labor trafficking, missing persons, narcotics, school
safety, and suspicious activity reporting. The Fusion Center also
inherited the residual analytical responsibilities of the Alabama
Homeland Security Office.[^24]

***ALEA Consolidation***

Governor Robert Bentley, elected in 2010 in the wake of the Great
Recession, sought a consolidated state-policing agency as part of his
campaign to economize state government spending. To this end, Governor
Bentley issued Executive Order 32, creating the Integrated State Law
Enforcement Task Force (ISLETF). He charged the task force with
identifying cost-saving opportunities within state law enforcement
operations.[^25] The ISLETF members included representatives from
Alabama's major state-level law enforcement employers, including the
Alabama Department of Homeland Security, the Alabama Department of
Public Safety, the Alabama Department of Conservation and Natural
Resources, the Alabama Department of Insurance (Fire Marshal's Office),
the Alabama Department of Revenue, and the Alabama Alcoholic Beverage
Control Board.[^26] ISLETF members were charged with identifying ways to
achieve operational cost-savings of at least 10 percent for their
departments by October 2012. Executive Order 32 concurrently provided
for creation of a supplementary analytical body, the Law Enforcement
Stakeholder Advisory Task Force (LESATF), which would evaluate proposals
put forward by the ISLETF for effect and feasibility.[^27] The findings
of the ISLETF---undergirded by the analysis of the LESATF---laid the
groundwork for the creation of the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency
(ALEA). Cost-saving recommendations, included, but were not limited to,
converting peace officer roles to civilian equivalents (e.g., fielding
Revenue Motor Vehicle Inspectors instead of Revenue Enforcement
Officers); developing resource-sharing practices of cross-trained
personnel and equipment; advocating a singular, updated state-wide radio
communications network; and practicing attrition-based retrenchment for
certain command roles.[^28]

The Alabama Law Enforcement Agency (ALEA) was created by Alabama Act
2013-67 (codified as The Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-21-1 eq. seq). It
represents the consolidation of law enforcement personnel and resources
formerly spread across twelve different state agencies. As of 2024 and
under the authority of the Secretary of ALEA, the agency is organized
along three divisions -- the Department of Public Safety, the State
Bureau of Investigations, and the Office of the Secretary. The
Department of Public Safety represents the uniformed officers of ALEA;
these officers serve primarily in patrol, security, and enforcement
roles. The State Bureau of Investigations encompasses ALEA's special
agents, who are primarily tasked with investigative and special project
roles. The Office of the Secretary houses most of ALEA's administrative,
legal, and support personnel. Together, the Department of Public Safety,
the State Bureau of Investigations, and the administrative apparatus of
the ALEA Secretary's Office work to fulfill most of the enforcement and
investigatory functions of state police power in Alabama.

During 2023, ALEA personnel worked more than 32,000 vehicle crashes,
inspected over 50,000 commercial motor vehicles, completed over 2,000
unmanned aerial vehicle flights, recovered 39 explosive devices, and
seized over \$37 million in narcotics. From traditional fingerprinting
and illicit still-busting to cutting-edge biometric iris captures and
generating three-dimensional crime scene models, ALEA draws on its
legacy agencies' experiences in deploying proven law enforcement
techniques while also demonstrating a willingness to adapt in order to
fulfill its ongoing commitment to protecting and serving Alabama's
citizens.[^29]

## Agency Organization {#agency-organization .H2}

The Alabama Law Enforcement Agency (ALEA) is headed by a Secretary, a
public official with "an extensive law enforcement background" appointed
by and serving at the pleasure of the Governor.[^30] Among other
responsibilities, the Secretary serves on the Governor's Cabinet and
acts as the State of Alabama's Homeland Security Advisor.

As of 2024, ALEA comprises three organizational subunits: the Office of
the Secretary, the Department of Public Safety, and the State Bureau of
Investigations.

***Office of the Secretary***

Reporting directly to the Secretary of the Alabama State Law Enforcement
Agency, ALEA's Office of the Secretary includes the Administrative
Bureau, Governmental Affairs Division, External Affairs Division,
Homeland Security Office, Integrity Unit, Legal Division, and Protective
Services Division.

The Administrative Bureau provides essential services to the entire
agency in the areas of Aviation, which assists with aerial surveillance,
fire suppression, and search-and-rescue missions using the agency's
fleet of airplanes and helicopters; Fleet Management; General Services,
including Facilities Management, Inventory Control, and Mail and Supply;
Information Technology; Personnel; and Radio Engineering.[^31] Staff
also support the Alabama Geographic Information Office, an operational
arm of the Alabama Geographic Information Executive Council that works
"to provide for discovery, access, exploration, and visualization of
geospatial data in a collaborative environment."[^32]

The Governmental Affairs Division liaises between ALEA and members of
the Alabama Legislature. Division staff review all law
enforcement-related legislation and develop ALEA's legislative agendas.

The External Affairs Division issues communications and provides
information about ALEA's operations to the public, media outlets, and
other law enforcement agency partners. Within the External Affairs
Division, the Photo Services Unit stores photographs and videos and
provides photography and videography services at ALEA events.

ALEA's Homeland Security Office collaborates with federal, state, and
local partner agencies to prevent and respond to terrorism in the state.
Among other responsibilities, staff may apply for, administer, and
regrant federal funds as part of initiatives like the Nonprofit Security
and State Homeland Security Grant Programs.[^33]

The Integrity Unit conducts administrative investigations following
incidents involving the use of potentially deadly force, or as otherwise
authorized by ALEA's Secretary or Deputy Secretary.

The Legal Division responds to public records requests and represents
the Secretary and ALEA personnel in federal, state, and administrative
litigation. As of 2024, the Division staff includes five attorneys and
three support personnel.

The Protective Services Division includes the Aviation, Capitol Patrol,
and Dignitary Protection Units. As provided by the Code of Alabama 1975
§ 32-2-100, the Capitol Patrol Unit employs officers "to preserve
order... \[and protect\] persons and property at the Capitol and all
\[other\] state buildings." The Dignitary Protection Unit provides
executive security to the Governor, Lieutenant Governor, Attorney
General, Speaker of the House, Senate President Pro Tempore, and the
Governor's immediate family.[^34]

***Department of Public Safety***

Pursuant to the Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-27-6 and after consultation
with the Governor, the ALEA Secretary appoints a Director of the
Department of Public Safety to "have overall supervision and management"
of the Department of Public Safety's functions. The department includes
the Driver License Division, Highway Patrol Division, Marine Patrol
Division, and Professional Development and Training Division.

The Driver License Division tests and maintains records on Alabama's
licensed drivers, including crash reports, traffic arrest forms, driver
license applications, and traffic-violation convictions.[^35] Within the
Division, staff are organized into the Commercial Driver
License/Medical, Driver Improvement, Driver Services, Hearings,
Mandatory Liability Insurance, Reinstatement, Records, and Safety
Responsibility Unit. The Alabama Law Enforcement Agency Driver License
System (LEADS) Help Desk is also housed within the Driver License
Division.

The Highway Patrol Division enforces motor vehicle laws, investigates
rural traffic crashes, and performs special duties as needed during
emergencies. ALEA empowers all sworn members of the Highway Patrol
Division to conduct traffic stops of motorists, issue warnings and
citations, and make arrests. Some Highway Patrol Division employees also
have additional duty assignments with the Honor Guard, K-9, Motor
Carrier Safety, Motor Enforcement and Traffic Homicide Investigation
Units. Also housed within the Highway Patrol Division, the
Communications Unit operates modern telecommunications centers providing
interoperable radio communications across the State of Alabama.[^36]

The Marine Patrol Division patrols Alabama's public waterways, oversees
vessel registration and operator licensing, and operates statewide
boating safety and educational programs. Marine Patrol Division troopers
are dedicated to work on waterways, rather than highways. Employees have
the same powers as Highway Patrol Division troopers but also possess
additional boating and marine safety qualifications.

The Professional Development and Training Division houses the ALEA
Training Center (ATC), a law enforcement training academy accredited by
the Alabama Peace Officers' Standards and Training Commission.[^37] The
ATC provides basic training to student officers from local law
enforcement agencies across Alabama, educates incoming state troopers,
and offers specialized courses, such as the Alabama Advanced Law
Enforcement Rapid Response Training (ALERRT) Program.[^38]

***State Bureau of Investigations***

As provided by the Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-27-5 and after consultation
with the Governor, the ALEA Secretary appoints a Director of the State
Bureau of Investigations to "have overall supervision and management" of
the State Bureau of Investigations' functions. The bureau includes the
Criminal Investigations Division, the Criminal Justice Information
Services Division, and the Alabama Fusion Center.

*\*\*Explanatory Note: Fusion centers promote information sharing
between federal, state, local, tribal, and territorial agencies. The
primary focus is to collect, verify, and analyze criminal justice
information, thereby transforming the data into actionable
intelligence.*[^39]

The Criminal Investigations Division conducts investigations and assists
city, county, state, federal, and international law enforcement agencies
in various criminal matters. The Division includes the Major Crimes,
Special Operations, Special Victims, and Vice Units. Teams within the
Special Operations Unit plan, budget, and implement new initiatives
"intended to keep SBI on the cutting edge of technology." As of 2024,
these initiatives include Crime Scene Digital Scanning, Digital
Forensics, Hazardous Device, Marijuana Eradication, Polygraph
Examination, Special Weapons and Tactics (SWAT), and Unmanned Aerial
programs.[^40]

The Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) Division is responsible
for "providing and maintaining mission-critical criminal justice
information systems and services" in Alabama.[^41] Staff offer training,
provide guidance, and monitor compliance with the criminal justice
information requirements outlined by the Federal Bureau of Investigation
and the Alabama Justice Information Commission.[^42] Within the
Division, staff are organized, as of 2024, into the Automated Biometric
Identification System (ABIS), Criminal History Repository, Criminal
Justice Compliance, Sex Offender Registry, and Uniform Crime Reporting
Program Sections.[^43]

The Alabama Fusion Center serves as a clearinghouse for intelligence
related to crime prevention and counterterrorism in the state. ALEA
staffs the Fusion Center with analysts who are subject matter experts in
one or more criminal justice fields including, but not limited to, cold
cases, cybersecurity intelligence, human trafficking, labor trafficking,
missing persons, narcotics, school safety, and Suspicious Activity
Reporting.[^44]

## Agency Function and Subfunctions {#agency-function-and-subfunctions .H2}

The mandated function of the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency is to
coordinate public safety in Alabama by overseeing state-level law
enforcement and investigative services.[^45] As such, it is one of the
agencies responsible for performing the Law Enforcement and Emergency
Powers function of Alabama government as described in the "Functional
Analysis of Alabama Government."

In the performance of its mandated function, the agency may engage in
the following subfunctions:

- **Maintaining Public Order and Security.** The Code of Alabama 1975 §
  41-27-1 outlines the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency's fundamental
  responsibility to "coordinate public safety in the state." ALEA
  fulfills this obligation in myriad ways; for example, the agency
  collaborates in varying capacities with other government bodies whose
  work pertains to statewide law enforcement, public safety, and
  homeland security. These include, but are not limited to, the Driver
  License Medical Advisory Board, Drug Enforcement Task Force, First
  Responder Wireless Commission, Geographic Information System (GIS)
  Executive Council, Homeland Security Task Force, and the Justice
  Information Commission.[^46]

> Pursuant to the Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-27-3.1, ALEA also has the
> authority "to apply for any grants and to accept any lawful gifts or
> donations" in the performance of its duties. To that end, ALEA
> administers several law enforcement and homeland security programs
> that are partially or fully grant-funded. As of 2024, ALEA serves as
> the state administrative agency for multiple Homeland Security Grants
> from the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA). The department
> may also administer grants on behalf of agencies including, but not
> limited to, the Alabama Department of Economic and Community Affairs
> (ADECA), the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA), and
> the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ).
>
> In accordance with the provisions of the Code of Alabama 1975 §
> 36-33-2, ALEA provides protective services to the Governor,
> Governor-elect, Lieutenant Governor, Attorney General, Speaker of the
> House, Senate President Pro Tempore, and the Governor's immediate
> family. The agency also houses the Capitol Patrol, a group of state
> troopers responsible for providing site security to the Capitol
> Building, Governor's Mansion, and other facilities within Alabama's
> Capitol Complex.

- **Licensing and Registering.** The Alabama Law Enforcement Agency
  (ALEA) is responsible for issuing driver licenses to Alabama
  residents.[^47] To be licensed, applicants must undergo vision
  screening; take an examination on Alabama traffic laws, road signs,
  and rules of safe driving; and pass a road skills test administered by
  a qualified examiner. The Code of Alabama 1975 § 32-6-3 empowers the
  Director of the Department of Public Safety to promulgate rules and
  regulations "as to the kind of examination or test to be given and the
  method and manner of giving the examination, ascertaining and
  reporting the results of the examination, and to who qualifies to give
  the examination." The agency also issues non-driver identifications,
  such as STAR ID.[^48]

> While boat registration is handled by county licensing officials
> acting on ALEA's behalf, localities forward the boat registration
> information to the Department of Public Safety's Marine Patrol
> Division, which audits (verifies the accuracy of) the information
> provided and makes it available for law enforcement. Similarly, county
> licensing officials send motor vehicle registration information to the
> Department of Public Safety's Driver License Division, ensuring that
> the information provided is accurate and readily accessible.[^49]

- **Enforcing.** The Code of Alabama 1975 § 32-2-5 empowers ALEA's
  Director of the Department of Public Safety "to do all that is
  necessary to administer and enforce" laws related to the operation or
  movement of motor vehicles on Alabama's public highways. To that end,
  Highway Patrol Division members have the powers of peace officers,
  granting them the authority to pull over drivers, issue warnings and
  citations, and make arrests.[^50] Marine Patrol Division employees
  review applications to install private aids-to-navigation, approve
  marine event permit applications, and inspect recreational vessels
  with marine sanitation devices installed on board, among other duties.

ALEA enforces the Alabama's Mandatory Liability Insurance Act, as well
as the nationwide safety requirements on behalf of the Federal Motor
Carrier Safety Administration. As part of its motor carrier safety
enforcement activities, the agency inspects commercial motor vehicles,
hazardous material haulers, and bus/passenger vehicles to ensure that
all applicable requirements have been satisfied.

*[Note:]{.underline}* *Commercial motor vehicles weigh 26,001 lbs. or
more and are used to transport passengers or property. Hazardous
material haulers transport hazardous materials as defined in 49 CFR §
172.101. Bus/passenger vehicles transport sixteen or more passengers,
including the driver, and weigh less than 26,001 lbs.*[^51]

Acting as a lynchpin to coordinate statewide law enforcement activities,
ALEA's Communications Unit is "is a modern telecommunications center
providing interoperable radio communications throughout the state of
Alabama." The Unit's staff of Police Communications Officers (PCOs) act
as emergency dispatchers for all ALEA departments, receiving and
forwarding information from the public, E911 centers, and local law
enforcement agencies.

- **Investigating.** ALEA's State Bureau of Investigations (SBI)
  provides investigative assistance to municipal, county, state,
  federal, and foreign law enforcement agencies. The agency may lead or
  consult on the identification, infiltration, investigation, and
  prosecution of individuals, career criminals, and organizations
  engaging in criminal activity tied to Alabama. Investigations may
  encompass such diverse matters as felony offenses, police use-of-force
  instances, agricultural crimes, and human trafficking. The agency also
  maintains a Special Weapons and Tactics (SWAT) Team and Hazardous
  Device Squad, both of whom deploy in instances warranting their
  specialized expertise.

> The agency utilizes investigative techniques including, but not
> limited to, interviews, materials testing, surveillance, digital
> forensics, and crime scene processing in the course of its
> investigations. For instance, ALEA's Unmanned Aerial Systems Unit
> responds to time-sensitive missions including, but not limited to,
> aiding in manhunts, assisting in marijuana eradication activities,
> providing aerial reconnaissance of certain crime scenes or crash
> sites, and search-and-rescue functions.

- **Collecting and Analyzing Information.** Through its Criminal Justice
  Information Services (CJIS) Division, ALEA's State Bureau of
  Investigations (SBI) is responsible for "providing and maintaining
  mission-critical criminal justice information systems and services" in
  the State of Alabama.[^52]

> ALEA participates in the federal government's National Crime
> Information Center (NCIC) and Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) programs,
> maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). Among other
> record types, the NCIC serves as the repository for criminal history
> record information (CHRI), defined in 28 CFR § 20.3 as "information
> collected... on individuals consisting of identifiable descriptions
> and notations of arrests, detentions, indictments, \[and\] formal
> criminal charges." The Department of Public Safety participates in the
> Fatality Analysis Reporting System (FARS), a nationwide census
> providing "yearly data regarding fatal injuries suffered in motor
> vehicle traffic crashes" to the U.S. Department of Transportation,
> Congress, and the public.[^53] These systems allow law enforcement
> jurisdictions across the country to collect, exchange, and analyze
> information relevant to public safety and criminal justice.

Additionally, ALEA operates the Alabama Fusion Center, a clearinghouse
for intelligence primarily related to crime prevention and
counterterrorism. Like other fusion centers across the country, the
Alabama Fusion Center provides actionable information, or intelligence,
to supplement investigative case files created and maintained elsewhere.
The Alabama Fusion Center creates and maintains databases to house
intelligence information related to cold cases, cybersecurity
intelligence, human trafficking, labor trafficking, missing persons,
narcotics, and school safety.

- **Educating and Promoting.** ALEA's External Affairs Division issues
  communications and provides information about agency operations to the
  public, media outlets, and other law enforcement partners. The agency
  also plans and implements educational programs targeted toward both
  law enforcement agencies and the general public.

Another of the agency's educational initiatives is the ALEA Training
Center (ATC), a law enforcement training academy accredited by the
Alabama Peace Officers' Standards and Training Commission.[^54] The ATC
provides basic training to student officers from local law enforcement
agencies across Alabama, educates incoming state troopers, and offers
specialized courses, such as the Alabama Advanced Law Enforcement Rapid
Response Training (ALERRT) Program.[^55]

- **Administering Internal Operations.** A significant portion of the
  agency's work includes general administrative, financial, and
  personnel activities performed to support the programmatic areas of
  the agency, including:

> **Managing the Agency:** Activities include internal office management
> activities common to most government agencies such as corresponding
> and communicating, scheduling, meeting, documenting policy and
> procedures, reporting, litigating, legislating (drafting, tracking),
> publicizing and providing information, managing records, and managing
> information systems and technology.
>
> **Managing Finances:** Activities include budgeting (preparing and
> reviewing budget package, submitting the budget package to the
> Department of Finance, documenting amendments and performance of the
> budget); purchasing (requisitioning and purchasing supplies and
> equipment, receipting and invoicing for goods, and authorizing payment
> for products received); accounting for the expenditure, encumbrance,
> disbursement, and reconciliation of funds within the agency's budget
> through a uniform system of accounting and reporting; authorizing
> travel; contracting with companies or individuals; bidding for
> products and services; and assisting in the audit process.
>
> **Managing Human Resources:** Activities include recruiting and hiring
> eligible individuals to fill positions within the agency; providing
> compensation to employees; providing benefits to employees such as
> leave, health insurance, unemployment compensation, workers'
> compensation, injury compensation, retirement, and death benefits;
> supervising employees by evaluating performance, granting leave, and
> monitoring the accumulation of leave; training and providing
> continuing education for employees; and investigating and
> disciplining.
>
> **Managing Properties, Facilities, and Resources:** Activities include
> inventorying and accounting for non-consumable property and reporting
> property information to the appropriate authority; leasing and/or
> renting offices or facilities; providing security for property managed
> by the agency; insuring property; and assigning, inspecting, and
> maintaining agency property.

[]{#_Toc445114424 .anchor}Records Appraisal of the Alabama Law
Enforcement Agency

The following is a discussion of the three major categories of records
created and/or maintained by the agency: Temporary Records, Permanent
Records, and Records No Longer Created.

[]{#_Toc1991552 .anchor}**Temporary Records**

Temporary records should be held for what is considered their active
life and be disposed of once all fiscal, legal, and administrative
requirements have been met. Some of the temporary records created by the
agency are discussed below:

**Maintaining Public Order and Security**

- **Grant Records -- Supporting Documentation.** ALEA administers
  several law enforcement and homeland security programs that are
  partially or fully grant-funded. For instance, as of 2024, ALEA serves
  as the state administrative agency for multiple Homeland Security
  Grants from the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA). The
  department may also administer grants on behalf of agencies including,
  but not limited to, the Alabama Department of Economic and Community
  Affairs (ADECA), the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration
  (FMCSA), and the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ).

Specific grant programs change over time, meaning the types of
supporting documentation associated with the programs may also change.
ALEA submits all necessary grant-related supporting documentation to the
relevant funding agency for processing. Grant-supporting documentation
should be retained for six years after submission of the final report or
conclusion of the grant, or two years after audit, whichever is longer.
The scope of this series does not include "Grant Project Final Narrative
Reports," which are appraised as permanent records and are described
below.

- **Protective Service Assessments and Reports.** In accordance with the
  provisions of the Code of Alabama 1975 § 36-33-2, ALEA provides
  protective services to the Governor, Governor-elect, Lieutenant
  Governor, Attorney General, Speaker of the House, Senate President Pro
  Tempore, and the Governor's immediate family. The scope of this series
  includes, but is not limited to, individualized security routine
  records and operations plans prepared before visits or events.
  Protective service assessments and reports should be maintained for
  two years to ensure they remain available for administrative
  reference.

- **Dignitary Protection Requests.** Federal agencies, state agencies,
  or international security services may contact ALEA to request the
  deployment of Alabama's protective service resources. As of 2024, the
  Protective Services Division leadership evaluates incoming requests
  and makes preliminary recommendations to ALEA's Secretary, who makes
  the final decision regarding which, if any, protective services may be
  offered. Dignitary protection requests should be maintained for two
  years to ensure they remain available for administrative reference.

- **Capitol Patrol Security Reports and Logs.** The Capitol Patrol is a
  group of state troopers responsible for providing site security to the
  Capitol Building, Governor's Mansion, and other facilities within
  Alabama's Capitol Complex. Records in this series document the Capitol
  Patrol's performance of these responsibilities, including, but not
  limited to, security logs and officer reports. Capitol Patrol security
  reports and logs should be maintained for two years to ensure they
  remain available for administrative reference.

Like all state troopers, Capitol Patrol officers have statewide
jurisdiction and are authorized to operate outside the Capitol Complex
if needed. Activities that do not specifically relate to Capitol Complex
security fall within the scope of "Enforcing" and "Investigating"
subfunction records, described in more detail below.

- **Protective Services Records Not Described Elsewhere.** This record
  series encompasses protective service files not represented elsewhere
  in the RDA. For instance, records related to non-standard protective
  service deployments, such as large-scale special events, fall within
  the scope of this series. These records should be retained for two
  years for administrative reference.

- **Aviation Records.** ALEA's Aviation Unit may deploy manned aerial
  resources for various purposes, such as prisoner extraditions, search
  and rescue operations, and assistance in law enforcement
  investigations. ALEA creates and maintains pilot logs, flight logs,
  aircraft maintenance records, flight requests, and scheduling files as
  part of its aviation program. It should be noted that geospatial
  flight data falls within the scope of the series "Ad Hoc Enterprise
  Activity Reports" and "Enterprise Geospatial Datasets Not Otherwise
  Described," discussed in more detail below.

Every pilot carries a personal pilot log while on duty, and every pilot
is responsible for updating their personal logbook. Pilot logs document
flight hours, medical clearance, flight certifications, and other
employee-specific information.[^56] To facilitate lookback by staff,
pilot logs should be retained for one year after the employee's
separation.

Every aircraft has a flight log onboard, and staff are responsible for
updating it whenever an aircraft is used or serviced. Flight logs and
associated aircraft maintenance records document total in-flight hours,
inspection status, maintenance work performed, and other
aircraft-specific information. These records should be maintained for
the duration of the aircraft's usage by the agency. If the aircraft is
sold, transfer records to the purchaser at the time of sale, as provided
by 14 CFR § 91.419.

The Aviation Unit acts solely in response to requests from other
federal, state, or local agencies. In these circumstances, the
requesting agency directs the Aviation Unit's actions and is responsible
for maintaining official records of the activities taking place. Any
flight request records or scheduling files created and maintained by the
Aviation Unit should be retained for two years to ensure they remain
available for administrative reference.

- **Motorist Assistance and Boater Assistance Records.** ALEA employees
  may, at times, assist motorists and boaters. For instance, a
  Department of Public Safety Highway Patrol Division officer may assist
  a motorist by bringing gasoline to a stranded driver. Motorist and
  boater assistance records should be maintained for two years to ensure
  they remain available for administrative reference.

**Licensing and Registering**

- **Driver License/Non-Driver Identification Files.** These files are
  created when someone applies for a driver license or a non-driver
  identification, such as a STAR ID.[^57] Hardship driver license files
  also fall within the scope of this series.[^58]

*[Note:]{.underline} The Alabama Administrative Code Chapter 760-X-1-.24
allows individuals to apply for a hardship driver license if they are
participating in a work release or Community Corrections program; have
recently been released from Alabama Department of Corrections (DCOM)
custody; or had their license suspended or revoked but cannot obtain
reasonable transportation. Hardship driver licenses allow driving for
work, school, doctor's appointments, emergency evacuations, and voting.*

These files contain information including, but not limited to,
applications; driver license examination scores; accident reports;
school dropout notifications; court corrections; insurance changes;
correspondence; and records documenting physical, medical, substance
use, and psychological issues that could impact the ability to drive
safely. Commercial Driver's License (CDL) medical certificates, which
document that a person has been medically cleared to operate a
commercial motor vehicle, also fall within the scope of this series.

Driver license/non-driver identification files should be maintained for
eighty-five years to ensure the records remain available throughout an
individual's entire driving history.

- **Driver License/Non-Driver Identification Photographs.** Driver
  license/non-driver identification photographs are maintained
  separately from other driver license/non-driver identification files.
  Licensing officials may take new photographs occasionally, and older
  photographs become less relevant once they no longer accurately depict
  their subject. Furthermore, since outdated photographs need not be
  maintained for as long as the identification files described above, a
  retention of twenty-five years is appropriate.

- **Motor Vehicle Record (MVR) Requests and Related Documentation.**
  Alabama is considered a "split state" in terms of motor vehicle
  licensing because one state agency (ALEA) is responsible for issuing
  driver licenses, while another agency (the Alabama Department of
  Revenue) registers and taxes vehicles. The Alabama Department of
  Revenue delegates motor vehicle registration responsibilities to
  county officials acting on the state's behalf. County officials
  forward motor vehicle registration information to the Department of
  Public Safety's Driver License Division, which audits (verifies the
  accuracy of) the information provided.

ALEA maintains Motor Vehicle Records (MVRs), defined by the federal
Drivers Privacy Protection Act, which pertain "to a motor vehicle
operator\'s permit, motor vehicle title, motor vehicle registration, or
identification card issued" by the Department of Public Safety's Driver
License Division.[^59] The agency may provide MVRs by request; since
there is an associated fee for MVR requests, the requests and related
documentation should be maintained for one year after audit.

- **Boat Registration Files.** Boat registration is handled by county
  licensing officials acting on ALEA's behalf. County licensing
  officials forward boat hull/serial numbers, registrant addresses, and
  other registration information to the Department of Public Safety's
  Marine Patrol Division, which audits (verifies the accuracy of) the
  information provided. Boat registrations must be renewed annually at
  the county level, so a retention of two years ensures that ALEA's
  files remain available for short-term administrative reference.

- **Commercial Driver's License Examiner Certification Records.** ALEA
  certifies everyone who administers Commercial Driver's License (CDL)
  examinations in Alabama. Some examiners are ALEA employees, while
  others work at community colleges throughout the state. Examiners must
  demonstrate proficiency with CDL-specific tasks, such as conducting
  pre-drive inspections of commercial motor vehicles and completing
  basic maneuvers in controlled environments. Certifications of ALEA
  employees should be transferred to the employee personnel file. To
  ensure the records remain available throughout an examiner's career,
  all other examiner certifications should be maintained for two years
  after the examiner becomes inactive.

- **Driver License Examiner Monitoring Reports.** The Department of
  Public Safety's Driver License Division monitors the activities of all
  driver license examiners, including Commercial Driver's License (CDL)
  examiners. Monitoring reports document that examiners are properly
  training commercial vehicle operators while following all applicable
  examination procedures. Since it is essential that monitoring reports
  be retained long enough to demonstrate patterns of examiner behavior
  over multiple monitoring cycles, these records should be maintained
  for three years.

- **Returned/Undeliverable Driver License Correspondence.** The
  Department of Public Safety's Driver License Division sends courtesy
  mail to drivers for various purposes. This series includes driver
  license correspondence or communications which are returned to the
  sender for any reason including, but not limited to, insufficient
  postage, incorrect address, forwarding order expired, or abandoned at
  a mail/document pickup station by a defunct addressee.
  Returned/undeliverable driver license correspondence that do not
  require follow-up or retention for other documentary purposes should
  be retained for sixty days to remain available for disputes.

- **Scrap Metal Recycler Registration Records.** According to the Code
  of Alabama 1975 § 13A-8-31.2, scrap metal recyclers must register with
  ALEA and pay annual registration fees to remain in operation. ALEA
  maintains a database of all registered scrap metal recyclers; the
  database contains information including, but not limited to, company
  names, addresses, phone numbers, counties of operation, and names of
  company administrators. Scrap metal recycler registration records must
  remain available as long as the recycler remains active. The files
  should be maintained for two years after the registration expires.
  Individual data fields may be replaced as superseded.

**Enforcing**

- **Warning Citations.** The Department of Public Safety's officers may
  issue warning citations for minor traffic violations at their
  discretion. Warnings have no associated fines, do not impact insurance
  rates, and do not appear on one's driving record. As of 2024,
  information is documented on Form AST-38 and housed within the
  electronic Mobile Officer Virtual Environment (MOVE) Suite. Warning
  citations should be maintained for three years to ensure their
  availability for review when multiple minor traffic violations occur
  over a short period.

- **Uniform Traffic Ticket and Complaint (UTTC) Records.** The Alabama
  Rules of Judicial Administration prescribe a standard Uniform Traffic
  Ticket and Complaint (UTTC) form to serve as the charging instrument
  "in all nonfelony traffic cases filed in all courts" in Alabama.[^60]
  In practice, all law enforcement agencies across the state use the
  UTTC to document traffic crimes and violations. Related records, such
  as payment confirmations for fines owed and notices of failure to
  appear in court, may also fall within the scope of this series. UTTC
  records are ultimately documented within the Unified Judicial System's
  electronic records repository.[^61] ALEA's copies of UTTC records,
  which the agency maintains solely for administrative reference, should
  be retained for five years.

- **Uniform Incident/Offense Reports.** The Alabama Justice Information
  Commission prescribes a standard Uniform Incident/Offense Report to be
  completed to document any incident or offense involving contact with
  law enforcement; as of 2024, this form is entitled AJIC-32. The report
  includes a narrative about the incident/offense and information about
  the involved parties and property. Related records, such as
  supplementary information captured via Form AJIC-33, also fall within
  the scope of this series.

ALEA participates in the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) program, a
nationwide statistical initiative coordinated by the Federal Bureau of
Investigation (FBI). Although the federal government maintains the
nationwide UCR repository, the State Bureau of Investigations (SBI)
Criminal Justice Information Services Division makes concurrent backups
of the information added by contributing law enforcement agencies in
Alabama. As such, Uniform Incident/Offense Reports ultimately flow into
the Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) Division's UCR
Database.[^62] Originating versions of Uniform Incident/Offense Reports
should be maintained for five years to ensure they remain available for
periodic Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and CJIS Division audits.

- **Uniform Arrest Reports.** The Alabama Justice Information Commission
  prescribes a standard Uniform Arrest Report to be completed when a
  person is arrested; as of 2024, this form is entitled AJIC-34. The
  report includes a narrative about the arrest, along with information
  about the arrestee and the charges they face. Related records, such as
  notices of failure to appear in court, may also fall within the scope
  of this series.

ALEA participates in the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) program, a
nationwide statistical initiative coordinated by the Federal Bureau of
Investigation (FBI). Although the federal government maintains the
nationwide UCR repository, the State Bureau of Investigations (SBI)
Criminal Justice Information Services Division makes concurrent backups
of the information added by contributing law enforcement agencies in
Alabama. As such, Uniform Arrest Reports ultimately flow into the
Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) Division's UCR Database.
Originating versions of Uniform Arrest Reports should be maintained for
five years to ensure that they remain available for periodic Federal
Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and CJIS Division audits.

- **Highway Patrol Non-Traffic Arrest Records.** The Department of
  Public Safety's Highway Patrol Division occasionally makes arrests for
  non-traffic-related matters; for instance, a vehicle may initially be
  stopped for a traffic violation, but if there are drugs in the
  vehicle, then an officer will make a non-traffic arrest for drug
  possession. Related records, such as notices of failure to appear in
  court, may also fall within the scope of this series.

ALEA participates in the federal government's National Crime Information
Center (NCIC), a centralized criminal justice information clearinghouse
maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). Among other
record types, the NCIC serves as the repository for criminal history
record information (CHRI), defined in 28 CFR § 20.3 as "information
collected... on individuals consisting of identifiable descriptions and
notations of arrests, detentions, indictments, \[and\] formal criminal
charges." Although NCIC hosts data uploaded from law enforcement
jurisdictions across the country, participating agencies remain
responsible for entering, modifying, and removing their own records as
needed.[^63] As such, arrest records ultimately become part of the
"Criminal History Record Information (CHRI) Database Entries,"
maintained by the State Bureau of Investigations Criminal Justice
Information Services (CJIS) Division.[^64] Originating versions of
Highway Patrol non-traffic arrest records should be maintained for five
years to ensure that they remain available for periodic audits and
agency reference.

- **Uniform Traffic Accident Reports.** The Department of Public
  Safety's troopers complete a standard Uniform Traffic Accident Report
  form to document the circumstances of a motor vehicle collision. As of
  2024, this information is documented on Form AST-27. The form includes
  information about all motorists, vehicles, passengers, and pedestrians
  involved in the collision, as well as diagrams and a narrative report
  of the incident. The scope of this series also includes Truck/Bus
  Supplement Reports, which employees append to a Uniform Traffic
  Accident Report if a vehicle weighing 10,001 lbs. or more was involved
  in the collision. To ensure that they remain available for any legal
  proceedings arising from motor vehicle collisions, Uniform Traffic
  Accident Reports should be maintained for ten years.

- **Warrant and Summons Records.** Warrants are court-issued directives
  authorizing law enforcement officers to undertake certain actions,
  such as arrests and property searches, while summons are forms used to
  notify defendants that they are required to appear in court.[^65] ALEA
  employees frequently serve and execute warrants and summons issued by
  courts. Warrant and summons recall orders, which document that a
  warrant has been rescinded and is no longer valid, also fall within
  the scope of this series. Since the official versions of warrant and
  summon records are housed by the originating court, ALEA's copies of
  the records need not be maintained beyond the immediate term. For this
  reason, warrant and summons records should be retained until executed
  or recalled.

- **Property Search Consent Forms.** Individuals sign these forms to
  grant consent for ALEA employees to search their vehicles or other
  property. When property searches lead to arrests, property search
  consent forms become part of the series "Uniform Arrest Reports,"
  described in more detail above. Property search consent forms that do
  not lead to arrests should be maintained for five years to ensure they
  remain available for administrative reference.

- **Notices and Affidavits of Intended Suspension of Driving
  Privilege.** These records document whether arrestees consented to
  chemical breath testing. Arrestees who decline to undergo chemical
  breath testing are understood to have given implied consent for the
  suspension of their driving privileges. The arrestee, officer, court,
  and Driver License Division all receive copies of the notice; as of
  2024, information is documented on Forms AST-60 and AST-61.

Driving privilege suspensions are ultimately documented within the
"Driver License/Non-Driver Identification Files," maintained by the
Department of Public Safety's Driver License Division. Driver
license/non-driver identification files should be maintained for
eighty-five years to ensure the records remain available throughout an
individual's entire driving history. For this reason, the Highway Patrol
Division's copies of notices and affidavits of intended suspension of
driving privileges need only to be maintained for five years.

- **Sobriety Examination Reports.** These records document the
  administration of field sobriety tests, which may include elements
  such as a horizontal gaze nystagmus check and a one-leg stand test. As
  of 2024, information is documented on Form AST-3. Sobriety examination
  reports are often completed alongside "Uniform Arrest Reports," which
  must be maintained for five years to ensure that they remain available
  for periodic Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and CJIS Division
  audits. Mirroring the retention for arrest reports, sobriety
  examination reports should also be maintained for five years.

- **Weapon Registration and Retirement Forms.** According to 27 CFR §
  479.104, law enforcement agencies must federally register all weapons
  acquired by nontraditional means, such as abandonment or forfeiture,
  and intended for official use. As of 2024, this information is
  recorded on Form 10 and submitted to the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco,
  Firearms, and Explosives. Because weapon registration and retirement
  forms may be subject to federal audit, the forms should be retained
  for one year after the audit following the weapon's retirement from
  active service.

- **Radar Equipment Recertification and Repair Files**. These records
  document the annual recertification of the agency's radar equipment,
  as well as any repairs performed on that equipment. Contemporaneous
  certification and repair information may be furnished when radar data
  is admitted into evidence by a court. Since these files have limited
  value beyond the near term, however, radar equipment recertification
  and repair files should be maintained for five years.

- **Radar Usage Logs.** These logs document each occasion that an ALEA
  employee utilizes a radar device. As of 2024, information is
  documented on Form AST-19. Radar logs should be maintained for three
  years to facilitate agency reference.

- **Traffic Safety Roadblock Program Reports, Inspections, and Other
  Records.** ALEA's traffic safety roadblock program utilizes interstate
  Combined Accident Reduction Effort (CARE) data and situational needs
  to plan and execute roadblock operations. Traffic safety roadblock
  program files may include, but are not limited to, vehicle checkpoint
  reports and vehicle inspection reports. As of 2024, this information
  is documented on Forms AST-8A and AST-8C. Traffic safety roadblock
  program reports, inspections, and other records should be maintained
  for three years to assist the agency in analyzing past and developing
  future traffic safety roadblock initiatives.

- **Wrecker Logs (Lists of Approved Wrecker Vendors).** The Department
  of Public Safety's Highway Patrol Division utilizes a list of approved
  wrecker vendors, also called a wrecker log, to request vehicle tows.
  Employees constantly cycle through the list, offering all tows to the
  next eligible vendor to ensure that all wrecker vendors receive equal
  opportunity. Wrecker logs should be maintained for three years so
  that, if concerns are raised, ALEA can demonstrate a fair distribution
  of towing opportunities.

- **Wrecker Vendor Inspection and Authorization Records.** To be listed
  on the Department of Public Safety's Highway Patrol Division wrecker
  log, vendors must first be authorized to interact with the public on
  the division's behalf. Wrecker vendors become authorized by receiving
  satisfactory criminal backgrounds and driving histories for all towing
  employees, as well as undergoing annual inspections of all equipment,
  premises, and vehicles. Wrecker vendor inspection and authorization
  records should be maintained for three years after last contact with
  the vendor so that ALEA can demonstrate that proper vendor
  authorization procedures are being followed.

- **Confiscated and Abandoned Vehicle Records.** The Department of
  Public Safety's Highway Patrol Division maintains records of
  confiscated and abandoned vehicles that have been towed. As of 2024,
  this information may be recorded on Forms AST-9, AST-32, AST-37, or
  CF-20. If a motorist calls to inquire about their vehicle's location,
  these records ensure that accurate information can be provided. A
  retention of three years ensures the records remain available for this
  purpose.

- **Hazardous Road Reports.** The Department of Public Safety's Highway
  Patrol Division utilizes these forms to communicate to local or state
  roadway infrastructure authorities the existence of dangers like
  potholes or roadway obstructions. As of 2024, information is
  documented on Forms AST-45 and DPS-45. Hazardous road reports need
  only be maintained for three years as the division forwards hazardous
  road reports to the Alabama Department of Transportation (ALDOT) and
  relevant county roadway authorities.

- **Radio Operator Activity Detail Database Entries.** When ALEA's
  Communications Unit receives notification of an incident, Police
  Communications Officers immediately begin documenting relevant
  information within an electronic record keeping system. For instance,
  traffic accidents are documented in an accident log table within the
  database which records the call date, call time, accident location,
  descriptions of the people and vehicles involved, and a unique
  system-generated call number. Since formal documentation of these
  incidents falls within the scope of other record series, such as
  "Uniform Traffic Accident Reports," radio operator activity detail
  database entries can be maintained for a relatively short, three-year
  timeframe.

- **Radio Operator Case Numbering Logs.** ALEA's Police Communications
  Officers assign Highway Patrol case numbers sequentially using an
  annual log. As of 2024, numbers include the four-digit year, Highway
  Patrol post identification number, and a sequential numerical
  identifier. As these logs are useful primarily for the purpose of
  assigning unique case numbers, radio operator case numbering logs need
  only be maintained for one year after they are superseded.

- **Daily Traffic Accidents and Activity Logs and Reports.** ALEA's
  Police Communications Officers prepare daily accident and activity
  logs and reports describing the daily number of motor vehicle
  accidents, injuries, and fatalities occurring by county. As of 2024,
  information is recorded on Form AST-20-A.

Accident and injury data is aggregated into annual *Crash Facts* reports
published by the Alabama Department of Transportation (ALDOT) in
consultation with the University of Alabama's Center for Advanced Public
Safety (CAPS).[^66] Fatality data is ultimately keyed into the Fatality
Analysis Reporting System (FARS), which is maintained by the National
Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) within the U.S. Department
of Transportation (described in greater detail below). Since this
information is captured elsewhere, daily traffic accident and activity
logs and reports need only be maintained for two years.

- **Trooper Shift Assignments.** These records document the work
  assignments, also called daily activity reports, given to troopers at
  the start of each shift. Supervisors prepare the shift assignments and
  hand them over to Police Communications Officers, who verbally explain
  them to troopers over the radio. As of 2024, information is recorded
  on Form AST-16. A one-year retention allows reference on which
  assignments troopers received.

- **Audiovisual Field Recordings.** These records may document
  interactions between Alabama Law Enforcement Agency (ALEA) employees
  and the public or may be created as part of the agency's routine
  public safety and investigative processes. Audiovisual recordings
  subject to this records retention include, but are not limited to, all
  content captured by body/vest cameras, in-car/dash cameras, and
  similar devices. The scope of this series may also include recordings
  that are exclusively audio or exclusively video.

Audiovisual field recordings that become part of case files should be
transferred to the corresponding investigation case file and retained
for the same timeframe as the rest of the file's contents. Recorded
information subject to a received complaint or records request, but does
not become part of a case file, should be retained for six months or
until the complaint or records request is resolved, whichever is longer.
Recorded information not subject to a complaint or records request and
does not become part of a case file should be retained for three months
to ensure its availability for short-term administrative reference.

- **Motor Carrier Safety Weight Reports****.** The Department of Public
  Safety's Highway Patrol Division enforces nationwide safety
  requirements on behalf of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety
  Administration.

*[Note:]{.underline}* *Motor carrier safety requirements apply to three
categories of vehicles*: *commercial motor vehicles, hazardous material
haulers, and bus/passenger vehicles. Commercial motor vehicles weigh
26,001 lbs. or more and are used to transport passengers or property.
Hazardous material haulers transport hazardous materials as defined in
49 CFR § 172.101. Bus/passenger vehicles transport sixteen or more
passengers, including the driver, and weigh less than 26,001 lbs.*[^67]

As part of these enforcement activities, the Division weighs commercial
motor vehicles using highway weigh stations and portable scales.[^68]
Vehicles that do not meet the weight requirements may be halted if they
are determined to pose a danger to other drivers. Since older reports
could be used to demonstrate unsafe driving practices over time, motor
carrier safety weight reports should be retained for five years. Tickets
or violations issued for failing to meet federal motor carrier safety
requirements fall within the scope of the series "Uniform Traffic Ticket
and Complaint (UTTC) Records," described in more detail above.

- **Motor Carrier Safety Inspection Records.** The Department of Public
  Safety's Highway Patrol Division enforces nationwide safety
  requirements on behalf of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety
  Administration. As part of these enforcement activities, the Division
  inspects commercial motor vehicles, hazardous material haulers, and
  bus/passenger vehicles to ensure that all applicable requirements have
  been satisfied. For instance, hazardous materials haulers must have a
  Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) on hand when transporting hazardous
  materials, while bus/passenger vehicles must carry valid insurance not
  only on the vehicle itself but also on the passengers. Vehicles that
  fail inspection may be halted if they are determined to pose a danger
  to other drivers. Since older inspections could be used to demonstrate
  unsafe driving practices over time, inspection records should be
  retained for five years. Tickets or violations issued for failing to
  meet federal motor carrier safety requirements fall within the scope
  of the series "Uniform Traffic Ticket and Complaint (UTTC) Records,"
  described in more detail above.

- **New Entrant Safety Audit Records.** The Department of Public
  Safety's Highway Patrol Division audits new trucking transport
  companies in Alabama that employ Commercial Driver's License (CDL)
  drivers. These new entrant safety audits ensure that the new company
  is in compliance with all federal equipment regulations. Motor Carrier
  Safety Unit staff complete these audits using standardized forms
  supplied by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA).
  Once a company passes a new entrant safety audit, the carrier will
  continue to be closely monitored for the rest of the new entry period,
  which lasts eighteen months. Provided that no subsequent safety
  problems are found, the company will receive permanent operation
  status at the end of the new entry period.[^69] A five-year retention
  encompasses the full new entry period and mirrors the retention for
  other Motor Carrier Safety Unit records within this document.

- **Reportable Accident Claim Records.** The Code of Alabama 1975 §
  32-7-5 defines a reportable accident as a motor vehicle crash in which
  someone is killed or seriously injured, or property damage in excess
  of \$250 occurs. If a reportable accident takes place without a law
  enforcement response, the involved parties must submit an accident
  claim form to the Department of Public Safety's Driver License
  Division to file a claim against an uninsured motorist. As of 2024,
  this information is recorded on Form SR-31. To ensure that they remain
  available for any legal proceedings arising from motor vehicle
  collisions, reportable accident claim records should be maintained for
  ten years. Accident reports submitted by the Department of Public
  Safety's Highway Patrol Division fall within the scope of the series
  "Uniform Traffic Accident Reports," described in more detail above,
  which are also retained for ten years.

- **Mandatory Liability Insurance Enforcement Records.** The Department
  of Public Safety's Driver License Division maintains various records
  pertaining to the enforcement of Alabama's Mandatory Liability
  Insurance Act.[^70]

Individuals and insurance companies may pursue financial recovery
against uninsured parties involved in motor vehicle accidents. ALEA
creates and maintains safety responsibility case files attached to
uninsured parties' driver licensure files; if uninsured parties do not
respond to court judgments made against them, the Safety Responsibility
Unit can sanction the parties' driving privileges. Records in this
subseries may include correspondence and conditional releases stating
that payment arrangements have been reached. The retention of ten years
is tied to how long the safety responsibility case files remain active
if no payment arrangement is made.

The law requires certain drivers to have high-risk insurance.[^71] Most
car insurance is on a vehicle, meaning that the vehicle will be insured
no matter who is driving it. By contrast, high-risk insurance is on a
person, meaning that the person will be insured no matter which vehicle
they use. Individuals subject to these requirements must submit
insurance verification reports to prove they carry valid high-risk
insurance. If drivers lose their statutorily required high-risk
insurance, then the insurance company will send notices of cancellation
or termination to the Driver License Division. The timeframe during
which a person must have high-risk insurance varies based on individual
circumstances, and records must remain available for agency reference
after the high-risk insurance requirement has ended. For these reasons,
high-risk insurance verification reports, notices, and correspondence
should be maintained ten years after high-risk insurance requirements
have ended.

- **Self-Insured Vehicle Insurance Change Records.** Individuals and
  businesses with more than twenty-five registered motor vehicles may
  self-insure rather than obtain motor vehicle insurance through an
  insurance company.[^72] Like other drivers, self-insured parties must
  provide proof of mandatory liability insurance when renewing their
  motor vehicle registration at county licensing offices. When a
  self-insurance policy changes, however, the self-insurer must also
  demonstrate to the Department of Public Safety that the new insurance
  complies with the mandatory liability insurance law. Self-insured
  vehicle insurance change records should be maintained for three years
  to ensure their availability for short-term administrative reference.

- **Agency Copies of Court Records Documenting Judgments, Orders, and

  impact driving privileges, as well as suspensions of driving
  privileges against Alabama drivers. Staff process sanctions from other

  documenting judgments, orders, and directives that impact driving

- **Driving Privilege Court Correction Requests.** When a court makes an
  error and suspends the driving privileges of the wrong person, the
  affected driver can request a correction through the Department of
  Public Safety's Driver License Division. The agency makes the
  correction after receiving a second court order to that effect, which
  falls within the scope of the separate series "Agency Copies of Court
  Records Documenting Judgments, Orders, and Directives that Impact
  Driving Privileges," described above. The scope of this series is
  limited to requests by drivers to correct their privileges in the
  Alabama Law Enforcement Agency Driver License System (LEADS). Driving
  privilege court correction requests need not be retained for as long
  as the court orders, so a retention of five years is appropriate.

- **Driving Privilege Appeal Case Records.** ALEA uses a point system to
  track moving violations committed by individual drivers, with high
  point values leading to the loss of driving privileges. Drivers may
  also lose driving privileges if they refuse to submit to a chemical
  breath test when suspected of driving under the influence. An
  individual may appeal the loss of driving privileges via the
  administrative hearing process. To initiate the process, a driver must
  first submit a hearing request on the ALEA website. At the conclusion
  of a hearing, the Driver License Division's Hearing Unit issues a
  summary decision to uphold or overturn the original decision ALEA
  made. Summary decisions can be appealed to a circuit court.

The scope of this series includes hearing requests and summary
decisions, which are the only original records created during
administrative hearings. Official documentation of driver license
suspensions, revocations, and restorations exists within the series
"Driver License/Non-Driver Identification Files," which are retained for
eighty-five years and described in more detail above. Driving privilege
appeal case records should be retained six years after the final
adjudication of the case, mirroring the retention for other routine
legal files.

- **Driving Privilege Mistaken Identity Letters.** The Department of
  Public Safety's Driver License Division handles cases of mistaken
  identity that can impact driving privileges. Individuals with
  multiple, severe driving violations may be flagged in the National
  Driver Register, which can unintentionally affect other drivers with
  the same name and birthdate.[^74] When ALEA becomes aware of such
  cases of mistaken identity, the Driver License Division sends a letter
  to the National Driver Register explaining the situation so that the
  mistake can be corrected. Driving privilege mistaken identity letters,
  also known as "Not Me" letters, should be maintained for two years to
  ensure that they remain available for reference until the National
  Driver Register has made the necessary corrections.[^75]

- **Marine Accident Investigation Records.** The U.S. Coast Guard tasks
  the Department of Public Safety's Marine Patrol Division with
  investigating vessel accidents that take place on Alabama waters.

- **Three Lakes Oversized Boat Records.** Alabama Act 2006-398, codified
  as the Code of Alabama 1975 § 33-6A-3.1, restricts the maximum length
  and speed of vessels on Lake Martin, R.L. Harris Reservoir, and Weiss
  Lake, collectively known as the Three Lakes.[^76] Although the
  restrictions took effect in 2007, the legislation allowed boats
  already on the lake to be "grandfathered" in, provided they maintain a
  continuous, valid permit and undergo annual inspections. The good
  standing of a Three Lakes oversized boat could be subject to question
  as long as the boat remains in use, so these permitting and inspection
  records should be maintained for the life of the vessel.

- **Marine Sanitation Device Inspection Records.** According to the Code
  of Alabama 1975 § 33-6A-6, recreational vessels with marine sanitation
  devices installed on board are subject to annual inspections.[^77]
  Both private marinas and the Department of Public Safety's Marine
  Patrol Division officers are empowered to complete these inspections,
  which may have an associated fee. Due to the fee collection, marine
  sanitation device inspection records should be maintained for two
  years after they are subject to audit.

- **Private Aid to Navigation Application Files.** A person or
  government agency may apply for a private aid-to-navigation to be
  installed in a waterway.[^78] The Department of Public Safety's Marine
  Patrol Division reviews applications, including proposed locations, to
  ensure that all necessary criteria have been met. Because private
  aid-to-navigation applications have limited administrative value after
  being approved or declined, private aid-to-navigation application
  files should be maintained for two years. Approved private
  aids-to-navigation will be documented in "Lists of Aids to
  Navigation," discussed in more detail below.

- **Lists of Aids to Navigation.** The Department of Public Safety's
  Marine Patrol Division maintains a list of all aid-to-navigation
  locations in Alabama, including the private aids-to-navigation
  discussed above and aids unilaterally placed by ALEA. The list
  includes the aid type (buoy or sign), aid description (color, shape,
  presence or absence of lights), and GPS coordinates. Precise
  geolocational data allows the Marine Patrol Division to identify
  whether an aid to navigation has been moved from its intended
  location. Because the agency updates these lists continuously as
  aids-to-navigation are installed, modified, and removed across the
  state, lists of aids-to-navigation should be maintained for the
  duration of administrative value. Individual data fields may be
  replaced as they become superseded.

- **Marine Event Permit Application Files.** Any event with spectators
  on or around a public waterway must be permitted by the Department of
  Public Safety's Marine Patrol Division.[^79] The permitting process
  allows the Marine Patrol Division to assess whether the event could
  cause any navigational or public safety concerns requiring further
  advance consideration. There are no fees associated with marine event
  permit applications. Because marine event permit applications have
  limited administrative value after the permitted events take place,
  marine event permit application files should be maintained for two
  years. Applications that become part of legal proceedings will become
  part of "Historically Significant (Special) Legal Files" or
  "Non-Special Legal Files," discussed in more detail below.

**Investigating**

- **Other Criminal (Non-Special) Investigation Case Files.** ALEA
  provides investigative assistance to municipal, county, state,
  federal, and foreign law enforcement agencies. The agency may lead or
  consult on the identification, infiltration, investigation, and
  prosecution of individuals, career criminals, and organizations
  engaging in criminal activity tied to Alabama. Files within ALEA's
  case management system may contain a variety of record types and
  formats including, but not limited to, standardized crime reporting
  forms, officer notes, diagrams, geographic information system (GIS)
  and three-dimensional measuring data points, documents originating
  outside law enforcement (such as hospital reports), investigative
  photographs, and investigative audiovisual recordings. The scope of
  this series encompasses investigative records created not only by the
  State Bureau of Investigations (SBI) but also by Department of Public
  Safety (DPS) divisions with investigative responsibilities, such as
  the Highway Patrol and Marine Patrol Divisions.

Death and major investigations constitute ALEA's inquiries into fatal
and major incidents, such as homicides with multiple victims and
use-of-force instances warranting criminal investigation. Most Class A
and Class B felonies fall within the scope of this series. Additionally,
this record series includes cold cases and investigations conducted by
the Department of Public Safety's Traffic Homicide Investigation (THI)
Unit. Death and major investigation case files should be maintained for
eighty years after final dispensation of the case to ensure that the
records remain accessible for post-conviction proceedings, which may be
filed at any time during an offender's incarceration.[^80] An
eighty-year retention also ensures that the investigative files remain
available if new leads or new evidence becomes available in unsolved
cases.

Intermediate criminal investigations constitute inquiries into criminal
matters of intermediate severity including, but not limited to, rural
crimes, simple drug possession, receipt of stolen property, and simple
theft. Most Class C and Class D felonies, as well as most Class A and
Class B misdemeanors, fall within the scope of this series. Intermediate
criminal investigation files should be maintained for twenty years after
the final dispensation of the case to remain available for agency
reference.

Low-level criminal investigations involve inquiries into criminal
matters of lesser severity including, but not limited to, disorderly
conduct, littering, and running red lights. Most Class C misdemeanors,
as well as traffic infractions and violations, fall within the scope of
this series. Low-level criminal investigation files should be maintained
for ten years after the final dispensation of the case, as all legal
appeals should be exhausted within this timeframe.

Permanent documentation of ALEA's investigative functions is captured
within the series "Historically Significant (Special) Investigation Case
Files," described in more detail below.

- **Evidence and Supporting Documentation.** These records include
  evidence in law enforcement custody that has not been released to its
  original owners. Disposition must be directed by written order of the
  court, following the provisions of Section D (Exhibits) of the Court
  Records Retention Schedules (2009 edition) published by the
  Administrative Office of Courts. Evidence and supporting documentation
  introduced as exhibits during court proceedings must be maintained for
  one year after final disposition of the case and until written consent
  for disposal is received from the district attorney and the judge who
  presided over the case. If the judge who presided over the case is

  obtained in lieu of the trial judge's consent. The same retention also
  applies to evidence and supporting documentation not introduced as
  exhibits during court proceedings.

Sexual assault evidence collections kits have different retention
requirements than other types of evidence. The Code of Alabama 1975 §
15-23-121 requires that sexual assault evidence collection kits,
associated test results (including DNA profile matches), and information
on the kit contents must be maintained "for at least 20 years." Upon
written request by the survivor, the items outlined above must be
preserved for an additional twenty years. Investigative files should be
maintained for the same timeframe as these items.

The Code of Alabama 1975 § 15-23-121 requires that sexual assault
evidence collection kits, associated test results (including DNA profile
matches), and information on the kit contents must be maintained "until
the survivor reaches 40 years of age if the survivor was a minor when
the \[sexual\] assault occurred." Upon written request by the survivor,
the items outlined above must be preserved for an additional twenty
years. Investigative files should be maintained for the same timeframe
as these items.

- **Confidential Informant Applications and Related Files.** ALEA
  regional offices can request permission to utilize confidential
  informants by submitting applications to the State Bureau of
  Investigations (SBI) headquarters. Any investigative findings from a
  confidential informant will be documented in the corresponding
  investigation case file. Since informants may resume working with ALEA
  after periods of inactivity, confidential informant applications and
  related files should be retained for seven years to ensure their
  availability for that purpose.

- **Crime Tip Records -- Unsubstantiated Tips.** ALEA's State Bureau of
  Investigations maintains telephone lines and email addresses dedicated
  to receiving tips from the general public. Substantiated tips
  warranting further investigation will be documented in the
  corresponding investigation case file. Records of unsubstantiated tips
  have little ongoing significance and should be retained for useful
  life.

- **Unmanned Aerial Systems Records.** The ALEA State Bureau of
  Investigations' Unmanned Aerial Systems Unit responds to a variety of
  time-sensitive, evidence gathering, and surveillance missions
  including, but not limited to, aiding in manhunts, assisting the
  Marijuana Eradication Unit, providing aerial reconnaissance of certain
  crime scenes or crash sites, and search-and-rescue functions. ALEA
  creates and maintains Federal Aviation Association (FAA) flight
  reports, flight logs, aerial system maintenance records, flight
  requests, and scheduling files as part of its unmanned aerial system
  program. It should be noted that geographic information system (GIS)
  and three-dimensional measuring data points, investigative
  photographs, and investigative audiovisual recordings are part of the

  and "Other Criminal (Non-Special) Investigation Case Files" described

  "Enterprise Geospatial Datasets Not Otherwise Described," discussed in

Flight logs, reports, and aerial system maintenance records document
total in-flight hours, inspection status, maintenance work performed,
and other aerial system-specific information. These records should be
maintained for the duration of the unmanned aerial system's usage by the
agency, mirroring the aircraft requirements outlined in 14 CFR § 91.419.

The Unmanned Aerial Systems Unit responds to requests from other
federal, state, or local agencies. In these circumstances, the
requesting agency directs the Unmanned Aerial System Unit's actions and
is responsible for maintaining official records of the activities taking
place. Any flight request records or scheduling files created and
maintained by the Unmanned Aerial Systems Unit should be retained for
three years to ensure they remain available for administrative

**Collecting and Analyzing Information**

- **Fatality Analysis Reporting System (FARS) Submission Files.** The
  Fatality Analysis Reporting System (FARS) is a nationwide census
  providing "yearly data regarding fatal injuries suffered in motor
  vehicle traffic crashes" to the U.S. Department of Transportation,
  Congress, and the public.[^81] ALEA staff key Alabama traffic fatality
  data into FARS from a variety of sources including, but not limited
  to, Uniform Traffic Accident Reports, death certificates, and drug
  test results. The public can access the national FARS repository
  online and view de-identified fatal crash information dating back
  to 1975. Although the federal government maintains the nationwide FARS
  repository, ALEA may create and maintain local copies of FARS
  submissions. These annual captures should be retained for three years
  to ensure that the data remains available if questions arise about
  ALEA's statistical analysis and methodology.

- **Intelligence Information Databases.** ALEA operates the Alabama
  Fusion Center, a clearinghouse for intelligence primarily related to
  crime prevention and counterterrorism. Like other fusion centers
  across the country, the Alabama Fusion Center provides actionable
  information, or intelligence, to supplement investigative case files
  created and maintained elsewhere. The Alabama Fusion Center creates
  and maintains databases to house intelligence information related to
  cold cases, cybersecurity intelligence, human trafficking, labor
  trafficking, missing persons, narcotics, and school safety.

Missing person alerts are also maintained within these databases,
including the America's Missing: Broadcast Emergency Response (AMBER)
Alert; Blue Alert; Emergency Missing Child Alert; and Missing and
Endangered Persons Alert. AMBER Alerts are issued when law enforcement
agencies have confirmed that a child under 18 years of age has been
abducted; the child is at risk of serious injury or death; and
sufficient descriptive information of the child, captor, or captor\'s
vehicle is available to issue an alert.[^82] The Blue Alert is issued
when a peace officer is missing, injured, or killed, and a suspect
remains at large who is believed to be a serious threat to the
public.[^83] The Emergency Missing Child Alert is issued when a child
under 18 years of age is missing, but not confirmed to be abducted, and
is believed to be in danger of serious injury or death. The Missing and
Endangered Persons Alert is issued when a person living with a
mental/physical disability, Alzheimer's disease, dementia, or Autism
Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is missing and at risk of severe injury or
death.[^84]

For all missing person alert types, local law enforcement agencies
initiate an alert by completing a form on the ALEA website. An analyst
reviews the form to ensure that the situation meets the alert criteria,
then prepares the official alert using two databases, one which
populates the data elements required by the National Crime Information
Center (NCIC) and another which stores media contact information.

ALEA participates in the U.S. Department of Homeland Security's
Nationwide Suspicious Activity Reporting Initiative.[^85] Members of the
public and members can submit tips to ALEA using a Suspicious Activity
Report form available on the Alabama Fusion Center's website. Suspicious
Activity Reports submitted through this initiative are housed in a
database that falls within the scope of this series.

As provided by 28 CFR § 23, fusion centers must adopt procedures that
"provide for the periodic review of information and the destruction of
any information which is misleading, obsolete or otherwise unreliable."
Intelligence information databases should, therefore, be maintained for
the duration of administrative value, and individual data fields may be
replaced as superseded.

> *[Note:]{.underline} Criminal intelligence information shall be
> maintained no longer than five years (28 CFR § 23.20).*

- **National Crime Information Center (NCIC) Hot Files.** ALEA
  participates in the federal government's National Crime Information
  Center (NCIC), a centralized criminal justice information
  clearinghouse maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI).
  As of 2024, the ALEA State Bureau of Investigations' Criminal Justice
  Services Information Division uploads twenty-two types of records,
  widely known as "hot files," into NCIC's central repository. There are
  seven varieties of property files: article, boat, gun, license plate,
  securities, vehicle, and vehicle/boat part. NCIC maintains fifteen
  types of persons files: extreme risk protection order, foreign
  fugitive, gang, identity theft, immigration violator, threat screening
  center, missing person, National Instant Criminal Background Check
  System (NICS) denied transaction, National Sex Offender Registry,
  protection order, protective interest, supervised release,
  unidentified person, violent person, and wanted person.[^86]

"Hot files" contain urgent information that must be immediately
accessible to law enforcement officers responding to incidents in
real-time, which means that the files have little ongoing significance.
Furthermore, although NCIC hosts data uploaded from law enforcement
jurisdictions across the country, participating agencies remain
responsible for entering, modifying, and removing their own records as
needed.[^87] For these reasons, National Crime Information Center (NCIC)
hot files should be maintained until superseded, concluded, or otherwise
resolved.

- **Annual Captures of Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) Database.** ALEA
  participates in the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) program, a
  nationwide statistical initiative coordinated by the Federal Bureau of
  Investigation (FBI). As of 2024, the UCR program intakes data via four
  reporting systems: the National Incident-Based Reporting System
  (NIBRS), which provides detailed records on 52 offense types; the
  Summary Reporting System (SRS), which furnishes aggregate offense and
  arrest data; the Law Enforcement Officers Killed and Assaulted (LEOKA)
  Program; and the Hate Crime Statistics Program.[^88] UCR entries can
  contain a variety of data points including, but not limited to,
  persons (wanted, missing, or unidentified); vehicles and boats
  (stolen, abandoned, impounded, recovered, or used to commit a felony);
  guns (stolen, lost, or recovered); articles (stolen, lost, or
  recovered); securities and traveler's checks (stolen or lost); and
  license plates (stolen or lost).

Although the federal government maintains the nationwide UCR repository,
the State Bureau of Investigations (SBI) Criminal Justice Information
Services Division makes concurrent backups of the information added by
contributing law enforcement agencies in Alabama. ALEA uses annual
captures of the uniform crime reporting database to prepare "Crime
Statistical Publications," which are appraised as permanent records and
described in more detail below. To ensure that the raw data remains
available if questions arise about ALEA's statistical analysis and
methodology, these annual captures should be retained for twenty-five
years.

- **Criminal History Record Information (CHRI) Database Entries.** ALEA
  participates in the federal government's National Crime Information
  Center (NCIC), a centralized criminal justice information
  clearinghouse maintained by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI).
  Among other record types, the NCIC serves as the repository for
  criminal history record information (CHRI), defined in 28 CFR § 20.3
  as "information collected... on individuals consisting of identifiable
  descriptions and notations of arrests, detentions, indictments,
  \[and\] formal criminal charges." Final case dispositions, including
  acquittal, sentencing, correction supervision, and release, are also
  notated in CHRI database entries.

Although NCIC hosts data uploaded from law enforcement jurisdictions
across the country, participating law enforcement agencies remain
responsible for entering, modifying, and removing their own records as
needed.[^89] CHRI database entries represent the "master" version of an
individual's criminal history report and must remain available
throughout an individual's lifetime; therefore, entries should be
maintained for eighty years after the last entry or until the
individual's death, whichever is sooner.

- **Requests to Review Criminal History Record Information (CHRI).** Law
  enforcement agencies and members of the public can request copies of
  criminal history record information (CHRI) for purposes ranging from
  verifying an individual's arrest history to conducting a
  pre-employment background check. Since a fee is associated with a
  request to review criminal history record information (CHRI), the
  requests should be maintained for two years after audit.

- **Biometric Identification Files.** ALEA's State Bureau of
  Investigations (SBI) coordinates and maintains Alabama's biometric
  repository, a person-centered information management system that
  contains iris scans; palmprints; fingerprints, both pressed and
  rolled; photographs of the face; and photographs of scars, marks, and
  tattoos.[^90] As of 2024, the biometric repository is entirely
  distinct from the agency's case management system, though staff may
  use both systems in tandem when dealing with a criminal matter. Like
  other criminal history-related record types, biometric identification
  files associated with criminal activity must remain available
  throughout an individual's lifetime; therefore, entries should be
  maintained for eighty years after the last entry or until the death of
  the individual, whichever is sooner.

Some biometric identification records are never associated with criminal
activity. For instance, biometric indicators may be uploaded when a
person undergoes a background check. Files can be reclassified from
non-criminal to criminal as needed, but biometric identification files
never associated with criminal activity should be retained twenty-five
years to facilitate in-house reference by staff.

- **Latent Print Records.** ALEA's State Bureau of Investigations (SBI)
  employs technicians who compare fingerprints located at crime scenes
  to fingerprints in the state's biometric repository, as well as other
  state and federal fingerprint repositories. Latent prints are
  typically unidentified when first taken into evidence. If ALEA
  confirms that the fingerprints match a specific individual, the
  identified prints and related information should be transferred into
  the associated biometric identification file (discussed above). Prints
  can be matched decades after being collected, so unidentified latent
  fingerprints should be maintained for eighty years.

- **Firearms Prohibited Person Database Entries.** Alabama Act 2021-246,
  codified as the Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-27-3.3, required ALEA to
  "develop, create, maintain, and administer a state firearms prohibited
  person database." The system serves as a central repository for
  convictions and orders that impact an individual's eligibility to
  possess a firearm under state or federal law. Firearms prohibitions
  can last indefinitely and must remain available throughout an
  individual's lifetime; therefore, firearms prohibited person database
  entries should be maintained for eighty years or until the death of
  the individual, whichever is sooner.

- **Ad Hoc Enterprise Activity Reports.** ALEA's State Bureau of
  Investigations (SBI) often supplies information, skilled personnel,
  and specialized equipment to support other agencies and external
  investigations. As a result, SBI generates investigative materials
  that usually reside in the case files of other federal, state, and
  local law enforcement agencies. Ad hoc enterprise activity reports
  provide a mechanism to record SBI's contributions that, otherwise, may
  only be reflected in case files belonging to the entities SBI assists.
  Investigation case files created and maintained by SBI, as well as
  other ALEA divisions, fall within the scope of the record series
  "Historically Significant (Special) Investigation Case Files" and
  "Other Criminal (Non-Special) Investigation Case Files," both
  described elsewhere.

Records within this series are general-purpose, flexible, and useful for
analyzing statistics and monitoring trends. Information captured within
these reports may include, but is not limited to, the number of arrests,
number of contacts, frequency of burglaries, number of use-of-force
instances, pounds of marijuana seized, and number of routine
communications exchanged within a given timeframe. Ad hoc enterprise
activity reports can take the form of narrative writeups, charts,
graphs, and web-based visualization tools like dashboards.

Because these reports are prepared sporadically and often by request,
the scope of this series does not include reports submitted to fulfill
statutory obligations, such as ALEA's quarterly reports to the Joint
Legislative Committee on Homeland Security Oversight. Instead,
statutorily mandated reports fall within the scope of the permanent
record series "Annual and Other Periodic Reports," described below.

Summary documentation of information found within ad hoc enterprise
activity reports can also be found within other permanent record series,
such as "Traffic Fatality Listings" and "Crime Statistical
Publications." Therefore, ad hoc enterprise activity reports should be
retained for useful life to facilitate in-house reference by staff.

- **Enterprise Geospatial Datasets Not Otherwise Described.** Many
  geospatial data points created and maintained within ALEA's Geographic
  Information System (GIS) are contextualized and linked to other record
  series; for instance, investigation case files and ad hoc enterprise
  activity reports contain geospatial data alongside standardized crime
  reporting forms, writeups, and statistical analyses. The scope of this
  series includes geospatial datasets not represented elsewhere in this
  RDA. Because these datasets have limited significance without
  associated contextual documentation, enterprise geospatial datasets
  not otherwise described should be retained for the duration of
  administrative value, and individual data fields may be replaced as
  superseded.

**Educating and Promoting**

- **Educational Training, Outreach, and or/Workshop Files -- Planning
  and Administrative Files (such as registration forms, payment
  information, and attendee lists).** ALEA staff offer various workshops
  and training opportunities targeted toward both law enforcement
  agencies and the general public. For instance, the ALEA Training
  Center offers basic police training on topics including, but not
  limited to, driving techniques and firearm handling. State trooper
  trainees enrolled at the ALEA Training Center undergo a more rigorous
  program with additional subjects, such as how to use radar, pepper
  spray, and tasers. The ALEA Department of Public Safety's Traffic
  Homicide Investigation (THI) Unit offers training on crash
  investigation techniques. An example of ALEA's non-law enforcement
  training offerings is the basic boating safety classes presented by
  the Department of Public Safety's Marine Patrol Division.

This temporary series includes routine administrative files associated
with offering these trainings, such as lesson plans, outlines,
assignment instructions, and attendance records. Boating basics
examination records, which signify that a person has passed a boating
safety class, also fall within the scope of this subseries. Planning and
administrative files should be retained for three years after the end of
the fiscal year in which the records were created to facilitate lookback
by staff. Information of enduring value about this training is captured
in the permanent record series "Educational Training, Outreach, and/or
Workshop Files -- Representative Packets, Presentations, and Resources,"
which is described below.

- **Law Enforcement Officer Trainee Records.** These records document
  the registration and performance of law enforcement officer trainees
  at the ALEA Training Center, along with the kinds of training
  received. As of 2024, local law enforcement officer trainees receive
  basic police training on topics including, but not limited to, driving
  techniques and firearm handling. State trooper trainees undergo a more
  rigorous program with additional subjects, such as how to use radar,
  pepper spray, and tasers. ALEA maintains individual training files for
  each trainee, including attendees who did not complete training. The
  scope of this series includes, but is not limited to, scores, grades,
  and instructor notes for all law enforcement officer trainees,
  including military veterans receiving tuition discounts for law
  enforcement training. Law enforcement officer trainee records should
  be maintained for seven years after the employee separates from law
  enforcement to mirror the retention for personnel files.

### Administering Internal Operations: Managing the Agency {#administering-internal-operations-managing-the-agency .H3}

- **Register of Administrative Rules.** According to the Code of Alabama
  1975 § 41-22-6 to § 41-22-7, agencies must compile and maintain "an
  official register of regulations which shall be compiled, indexed,
  published in loose-leaf form, electronic form, or both, and kept up to
  date..." This "official register of regulations" is known as "The
  Alabama Law Enforcement Agency Code" and must be made available upon
  request to all persons. Since the register must be updated on an
  ongoing basis, it is to be retained until superseded.

- **Administrative Rule Change Working Files.** The Alabama
  Administrative Procedures Act (Code of Alabama 1975 Title 41
  Chapter 22) details the steps which an agency must follow when
  updating its administrative rules. Working files are produced in the
  course of the proposal, adoption, amendment, and/or repeal of
  administrative rules. Records in this series may include, but are not
  limited to, the following: received public comments, economic impact
  statements, and business impact analyses. A retention of one year
  after adoption, amendment, repeal, or rejection of an administrative
  rule permits review by staff when considering similar rules.

<!-- -->

- **Open Records Requests and Related Documentation.** This series
  encompasses requests to view records in accordance with Open Records
  laws and any related correspondence or other documentation related to
  the fulfillment or denial of the request(s). Open records requests and
  related documentation should be retained for three years. If
  litigation is initiated during this period, records should be
  maintained for one year after litigation concludes.

- **Administrative Investigation Files.** The administrative
  investigation process determines whether ALEA employees have violated
  agency policy. Administrative investigations may be initiated at the
  direction of the ALEA Secretary or Deputy Secretary, or in
  circumstances where a high-level use of force has occurred.[^91] While
  the agency primarily conducts administrative investigations of ALEA
  employees, in certain cases, the Secretary may direct ALEA staff to
  conduct administrative investigations on behalf of other government
  agencies. Administrative investigators collect evidence, interview
  witnesses, compile relevant documentation, and ultimately conclude
  with one or more findings. In accordance with the Code of Alabama 1975
  § 36-21-55.1, administrative investigation files should be maintained
  for 12 years after separation of the employee(s) subject to
  investigation.

<!-- -->

- **Local Law Enforcement Agency Compliance Audit Files.** ALEA's
  Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) Division conducts
  compliance audits of all local law enforcement agencies. There are
  approximately nine different types of audits that the Compliance Unit
  performs on local law enforcement agencies. Some audits require highly
  specialized training to perform, while others are more generalized.
  Subject matter ranges from physical security, such as locking doors
  and securely destroying old computers, to policy, such as conducting
  background checks on employees with access to sensitive information.
  As of 2024, ALEA conducts compliance audits on all local law
  enforcement agencies approximately every three years; the Federal
  Bureau of Investigation (FBI) audits a random sample of local law
  enforcement agencies during their statewide audits of the CJIS
  Division. Since the audits need to be retained long enough to
  demonstrate patterns of agency conduct over multiple compliance audit
  cycles, local law enforcement agency compliance audit files should be
  maintained for nine years.

- **Law Enforcement Information Technology System Support Platform
  Tickets and Requests.** The agency's Information and Technology Unit
  maintains software systems used by ALEA and its federal, state, and
  local law enforcement agency partners. ALEA utilizes an information
  technology (IT) management platform to receive and process "tickets"
  related to these systems. Different types of tickets include various
  categories of information including, but not limited to, requestor
  name, document code, impact, department, urgency, and incident
  description. These tickets should be retained for seven years in
  accordance with the retentions for similar records created by other
  agencies, such as the Alabama Department of Finance and the Office of
  Information Technology (OIT).

- **Law Enforcement Information Technology System Support Platform User
  Information.** The agency's Information and Technology Unit maintains
  software systems used by ALEA and its federal, state, and local law
  enforcement agency partners. ALEA utilizes an information technology
  (IT) management platform to receive and process "tickets" related to
  these systems. Information is produced and maintained pertaining to
  users who interact with the tickets on the platform. For example, the
  platform may store private messages sent by a user and a list of work
  groups to which the user belongs. To ensure the availability of user
  information for as long as the support platform is used, user
  information should be retained for seven years after the user becomes
  inactive, and other data entry fields until superseded. The seven-year
  retention corresponds with the retentions for similar records created
  by other agencies, such as the Alabama Department of Finance and the
  Office of Information Technology (OIT).

- **Law Enforcement Agency-Owned Animal Records.** These records
  document the purchase, training, ownership, care, and deployment of
  animals owned by the law enforcement agency. Examples of such animals
  may include, but are not limited to, K9 dogs, cadaver dogs, hazardous
  device detection dogs, drug detection dogs, and search and rescue
  dogs. Records in this series may include, but are not limited to,
  animal purchasing records, training certifications, animal microchip
  information, use and assignment records, and animal veterinary
  records. Mirroring the retention of employee personnel files, law
  enforcement agency-owned animal records should be maintained for seven
  years after the animal's retirement, death, or transfer from the
  agency.

- **Non-Special Legal Files.** ALEA's Legal Division staff may create
  and maintain briefs, writs, and other legal working files related to
  civil and administrative proceedings. Non-special legal files should
  be maintained for six years after the final dispensation of the case.
  Permanent documentation of ALEA's historically significant legal
  proceedings is captured within the series "Historically Significant
  (Special) Legal Files," described in more detail below.

- **Interagency Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs).** ALEA may enter into
  memoranda of understanding (MOUs) with federal, state, and local law
  enforcement agencies related to coordinating criminal justice
  activities in Alabama. For instance, the Department of Public Safety's
  Marine Patrol Division is party to a memorandum of understanding with
  the U.S. Coast Guard; among other provisions, the document outlines
  the division of responsibilities between the two agencies related to
  aids-to-navigation.[^92] Given that memoranda of understanding may
  involve the expenditure of government funds, a records retention of
  six years after expiration or termination of the agreement, or one
  year after audit, whichever is longer, complies with the statute of
  limitations for charges related to the misuse of state funds, as
  outlined by the Alabama Attorney General's Office.

### Administering Internal Operations: Managing Finances {#administering-internal-operations-managing-finances .H3}

- **Budgeting Records.** These records include, but are not limited to,
  budget development files, budget requests, operations plans, quarterly
  performance reports, appropriation and allotment revisions, and
  supporting documentation. As recommended by the Examiners of Public
  Accounts, budgeting records should be maintained for one year after
  compliance or financial audit by the Examiners of Public Accounts or
  an equivalent auditing firm or one year after the end of the fiscal
  year the audit covers, whichever is later.

- **Routine Accounting Records.** These are records of original entry or
  other routine accounting transactions including, but not limited to,
  journals, check registers, ledgers, bank statements, deposit slips,
  canceled checks, point-of-sale receipts and reports, and supporting
  documentation. As recommended by the Examiners of Public Accounts,
  routine accounting records should be maintained for one year after
  compliance or financial audit by the Examiners of Public Accounts or
  an equivalent auditing firm or one year after the end of the fiscal
  year the audit covers, whichever is later.

> *[Note:]{.underline} Data in the State of Alabama Accounting and
> Resource System (STAARS) is owned jointly by state agencies and the
> Department of Finance and administered by the Department of Finance's
> State Business Systems (SBS) Division. Records housed within STAARS
> are maintained for seven years in accordance with the Department of
> Finance's RDA.*

- **Procurement Records.** Records within this series include, but are
  not limited to, requisitions, receipts, invoices, purchase orders, and
  payment authorizations for goods and services. As recommended by the
  Examiners of Public Accounts, procurement records should be maintained
  for one year after compliance or financial audit by the Examiners of
  Public Accounts or an equivalent auditing firm or one year after the
  end of the fiscal year the audit covers, whichever is later.

- **Bid Files.** These records include, but not are limited to,
  invitations to bid, requests for proposals, all solicitation
  responses, contracts, master agreements, terminations, and related
  files. In accordance with guidance received from the Department of
  Finance's Division of Procurement, bid files should be maintained for
  seven years after the end of the contract.

- **Travel Records.** These records document requests, authorizations,
  and reimbursement records for travel, lodgings, and meals.
  Supplementary documentation, such as itineraries and travel receipts,
  also fall within the scope of this series. As recommended by the
  Examiners of Public Accounts, travel records should be maintained for
  one year after compliance or financial audit by the Examiners of
  Public Accounts or an equivalent auditing firm or one year after the
  end of the fiscal year the audit covers, whichever is later.

- **Ad Hoc Financial Reports.** The scope of this record series includes
  miscellaneous, nonstandard financial reports created for operational
  purposes. Because these financial reports have limited utility beyond
  the immediate term, they should be maintained for their useful life.

- **Agency Copies of Audit Reports.** Agencies may receive copies of
  audit reports created by the Examiners of Public Accounts or
  equivalent auditing entities. Audit reports should be retained long
  enough to demonstrate patterns of behavior over multiple audit cycles;
  therefore, agency copies of audit reports should be retained until the
  completion and release of two successive audit reports.

- **Debt Collection Records.** This series includes records that
  document an agency's efforts to collect unpaid accounts and that fall
  outside the scope of routine accounting records. Consistent with the
  statute of limitations for commencing a civil action for breach of
  contract, debt collection records should be maintained for six years
  after the account paid in full or deemed uncollectible.[^93]

- **Board of Adjustment Files.** These materials relate to an agency's
  involvement in Board of Adjustment proceedings, and include, but are
  not limited to, claims, supporting documentation, findings, decisions,
  and final orders issued by the Board. Mirroring the retention for
  claim files maintained by the Board of Adjustment, agency copies of
  Board of Adjustment files should be maintained for three years after
  final disposition of claim.

- **Tax Returns, Forms, and Related Files.** These records are local,
  state, and federal tax information generated by an employer and may
  include, but are not limited to, documentation such as Forms W-2, W-3,
  1094, 1095, 1098, and 1099 and other reports. Agencies may furnish
  this information to a local, state, or federal government agency, such
  as the Internal Revenue Service (IRS), or other parties to the
  transactions. Tax returns, forms, and related files should be
  maintained for seven years after the record was created in accordance
  with best practices in the accounting profession.

- **Sales Tax Exemption Documentation.** Government agencies are legally
  exempted from paying sales tax per the Code of Alabama 1975 §
  40-23-49. The Alabama Department of Revenue may provide proof of
  sales-tax-exempt status on request. Aligning with the retention for
  the Department of Revenue's corresponding records, sales tax exemption
  documentation should be maintained for six years after the end of the
  fiscal year in which the record was created.

- **Investment Files.** Some agencies may buy, sell, and report on
  investments including, but not limited to, bonds, equities, mutual
  funds, real estate, and securities. Original investment files may
  remain in the custody of third-party investment companies. Records
  documenting proof of purchase of investment should be maintained for
  seven years after liquidation of agreement based on best practice in
  the investment profession.

Investment management records and reports document the selection,
procurement, contribution, transaction, and distribution of investments.
Records within the scope of this series may include, but are not limited
to, selection criteria, prospectus, research working materials, periodic
statements, correspondence, projections, end-of-year summary account
statements, and annual or other periodic reports. In accordance with the
retentions for similar records created and maintained by the State
Treasurer's Office, these records should be maintained for seven years
after the record was created.

### Administering Internal Operations: Managing Human Resources {#administering-internal-operations-managing-human-resources .H3}

- **Job Recruitment Materials.** These records document efforts to
  advertise positions and attract qualified personnel and should be
  retained for three years.

- **Job Application Materials.** These records document applications for
  employment, including both merit and non-merit positions. Successful
  applications and appointment letters should be transferred to the
  employee personnel file. Successful applicants' supplementary
  documentation, which may include cover letters, resumes, transcripts,
  pre-employment drug testing results, and letters of recommendation,
  should be maintained for three years. Unsuccessful applications and
  supplementary documentation, which also include unsolicited and
  incomplete applications, should also be maintained for three years.

- **I-9 and E-Verify Forms.** This record series consists of information
  used by employers to verify the identity and employment authorization
  of individuals being hired. Records in this series should be
  maintained for seven years after separation of the employee from the
  agency to mirror the retention for personnel files. This retention
  encompasses both the audit requirements of the Examiners of Public
  Accounts and federal regulations.

- **Agency Copies of Personnel Registers, Certifications, and Related
  Files.** Agencies may receive copies of documents created and
  maintained by the State Personnel Department during the hiring
  process. For instance, the State Personnel Department may provide a
  copy of the certification (also called the certificate of eligibles),
  a list of the top applicants on the employment register at a given
  time; or a position allocation report, an overview of which job
  classifications are allocated to the department and whether they are
  vacant or filled. The State Personnel Department recommends that
  certificates of eligibles be retained in the agency's employee
  personnel file as documentation of appointment. However, the official
  versions of employment registers, certifications, state
  classifications, and pay plans are maintained by the State Personnel
  Department, so these records need only be maintained for one year.

- **Employee Personnel Files.** Information in employee personnel files
  may include copies of state applications, appointment letters, and
  records concerning personnel action changes, such as hiring,
  termination, retirement, and resignation. The scope of the series also
  includes personnel files for contracted workers (contractors) and paid
  interns. Official personnel files should be maintained seven years
  after separation of the employee from the agency, while
  department/division personnel files should be maintained one year
  after separation of the employee from the department or division.

- **Employee-Specific Position Classification Questionnaires.** The
  State Personnel Department recommends, but does not require, that
  state agencies maintain Position Classification Questionnaires (also
  called PCQs or Form 40s) for each employee at the agency level. These
  forms document responsibilities assigned to individual employees,
  which may vary between employees with the same job title and should be
  maintained for seven years after the separation of the employee from
  the agency to mirror the retention for personnel files.

- **Job-Specific Position Classification Questionnaires.** Some state
  agencies create a single Position Classification Questionnaire (also
  called a PCQ or Form 40) for each employee classification under their
  purview. These forms, which document responsibilities carried out by
  groups of employees with the same classification, should be maintained
  until superseded or until the position is abolished.

- **Employee Performance Appraisals.** These records document the
  evaluation of employee job performance. Records in this series may
  include, but are not limited to, pre-appraisals, mid-appraisals, and
  agency copies of annual appraisals. Employee performance appraisals
  should be maintained for seven years after the separation of the
  employee from the agency to mirror the retention for personnel files.

- **Employee Payroll Deduction Authorizations.** These records document
  voluntary and involuntary payroll deduction authorizations by the
  employee, by a court, or by other entities. These deductions could
  include, but are not limited to, deferred compensation, charitable
  contributions, garnishments, and tax withholding (as documented by W-4
  forms). The scope of this series includes records related to the
  commencement, modification, or termination of deductions.

Payroll deduction authorizations may or may not be kept with personnel
files, and they may or may not last for the duration of employment. The
Internal Revenue Service (IRS)'s Publication 15 states that employers
should keep "records of employment taxes," including copies of income
tax withholding certificates, for at least four years. Since payroll
deduction authorizations are important for the duration of the
withdrawal, but a deduction authorization could last less than one year,
a retention of four years after termination of the deduction covers both
administrative needs and federal requirements.

- **Employee Payroll Records -- Payroll records essential to calculating
  retirement.** Records within this series are necessary to ensure
  proper payment of state employees' retirement benefits and may
  include, but are not limited to, pay rates, total compensation, and
  hours worked. Payroll records essential to calculating retirement
  should be retained seventy-five years, or four years after retirement,
  whichever is shorter; these records are maintained by the State
  Comptroller for the same timeframe. The scope of this series applies
  to equivalent payroll systems, but not payroll records that are
  already captured within the Government Human Resources System (GHRS).
  This retention ensures that the records remain available until the
  employee retires.

- **Employee Payroll Records -- All other payroll records.** This series
  encompasses records generated in the course of running payroll, but
  which are not necessary to calculate employee retirement benefits.
  These records should be retained for seven years based on best
  practices in the accounting profession.

- **Complaints Against Employees.** This record series consists of
  complaints against individual employees made by members of the public
  or other employees. The three-year retention allows sufficient time to
  document trends in complaints against employees. Some employees by
  nature of their position receive many complaints, many of which are
  unfounded. However, some complaints may be legitimate enough to be
  placed in the employee disciplinary record. The scope of this series
  only encompasses complaints that do not result in formal disciplinary
  action, which instead fall within the scope of "Employee Disciplinary
  Records," described in more detail below.

- **Employee Disciplinary Records.** This record series documents
  disciplinary actions against an employee. Records in this series may
  include, but are not limited to, complaints against the employee
  leading to disciplinary action, written warnings, reprimands,
  documentation, statements by the employee, action plans, requests for
  mandatory leave, administrative hearing files, and appeals. The
  records should be maintained for seven years after separation of the
  employee from the agency to mirror the selected retention for employee
  personnel files.

- **Records Documenting Formal Grievances.** This record series
  documents agency proceedings in the settlement of disputes within an
  agency. Grievances are not necessarily employee-specific and may not
  relate to the disciplinary process. Records documenting formal
  grievances should be maintained for six years after resolution, in
  keeping with the retention for other legal case files. Grievances that
  result in employee disciplinary actions should be retained in
  accordance with "Employee Disciplinary Records," described in more
  detail above.

- **Work Schedule Records.** This record series documents employees'
  daily and weekly work schedules, as well as shift assignments.
  Scheduling records for volunteers and interns also fall within the
  scope of this series. These records have short-term administrative
  value and should be maintained for one year.

- **Activity Reports.** These records document work and duty
  assignments, as well as employee activity reports submitted on a
  periodic basis (such as weekly, monthly, or quarterly). Reports
  prepared by supervisors that document employee activities also fall
  within the scope of this series. These records have short-term
  administrative value and should be maintained for one year.

- **Employee Leave and Attendance Records.** These records document
  hours worked (including overtime), leave earned, leave requested, and
  leave taken by employees and often take the form of time sheets. The
  scope of this series also includes records related to other types of
  leave including, but not limited to, mandatory military service, Leave
  Without Pay (LWOP), donated leave, and jury duty. Records documenting
  the Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA) program do not fall within the
  scope of this series. These records must remain available until
  audited by the Examiners of Public Accounts or another equivalent
  auditing body, so they should be retained for one year after audit.

- **Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA) Records.** These records document
  the agency's compliance with the provisions of the Family Medical
  Leave Act (FMLA). Records in this series may include, but are not
  limited to, employee requests to take leave, medical documentation,
  correspondence, and records pertaining to re-certification or
  extension of leave time. These records should be maintained for three
  years to comply with the federal requirements outlined in 29 CFR §
  825.500.

- **Volunteer Service Files.** These files may include applications,
  medical release forms, and signed volunteer policies and procedures.
  Information captured in the files includes, but is not limited to, the
  volunteer's name, contact information, assignment(s), and years of
  service. These records should be retained for two years after the
  volunteer becomes inactive to account for the entire duration of a
  volunteer's service while also accounting for gaps in service.

<!-- -->

- **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) Compliance Records.**
  These records document charges of discrimination filed against an
  agency, statements of position, notices from the federal U.S. Equal
  Employment Opportunity Commission, all other filings and attachments
  related to EEOC complaints or allegations, and reports classifying
  employees by race and gender as provided by federal EEOC rules. These
  records should be maintained for three years in accordance with
  federal regulations.

- **Unemployment Compensation Reports.** State agencies must submit
  employee wage and tax reports to the Alabama Department of Labor on a
  quarterly basis, which are used to determine unemployment tax rates
  and to calculate how much unemployment compensation should be paid to
  individual claimants. These records should be maintained for seven
  years to conform with industry standard practices.

- **Unemployment Tax Payment Records.** State agencies must pay taxes
  into the Unemployment Compensation Trust Fund on a quarterly basis.
  These records should be maintained for seven years to conform with
  industry standard practices.

- **Unemployment Claim Response Records.** The Alabama Department of
  Labor provides written notice to the former employer when an
  unemployment compensation claim has been filed. The employer must then
  provide additional information about the individual's separation.
  These records should be maintained for seven years to conform with
  industry standard practices.

- **Employee Professional Development Records.** These records include
  documentation of an employee's training, professional development, or
  continuing education completed and submitted during employment. They
  may include, but are not limited to, certifications, transcripts, test
  scores, course descriptions, attendance verifications, and training or
  conference agendas. Records required for hiring, promotion, or
  continuing employment should be maintained for seven years after
  separation of the employee from the agency to mirror the chosen
  retention for personnel files. All other professional development
  records have short-term administrative value and should be maintained
  for three years.

- **Reports of Individuals Required to File Statements of Economic
  Interest.** These records consist of reports submitted to the Alabama
  Ethics Commission that list the names of individuals required to file
  Statements of Economic Interest. These records should be maintained
  for four years to encompass the statute of limitations for state
  ethics law violations.

- **Agency Copies of Affordable Care Act (ACA) Compliance Files.** These
  records encompass the documentation submitted to the federal
  government to demonstrate compliance with the Affordable Care Act.
  Federal requirements dictate that these records be maintained for
  three years after submission.

- **Workers' Compensation Records.** These records document state agency
  activities pertaining to State Employee Injury Compensation Trust Fund
  (SEICTF) claims. Records in this series may include first report of
  injury forms, accident employee statements, worker accommodations
  stemming from injuries, work-related injury time-off requests,
  documentation related to election of benefits, and related files. As

- **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Accommodation Documentation.**
  These records document requests for and provision of reasonable
  accommodations in accordance with the Americans with Disabilities Act

  employee requests, supporting medical documentation, correspondence,
  work plans, and related files. These records should be maintained for
  two years after the accommodation ends or request is denied. If the
  employee is terminated, retain records for two years after termination
  of the employee as provided by 29 CFR § 1602.14.

- **Exposure to Hazardous Substances Compliance Files.** These records
  document the agency's compliance with regulations promulgated by the
  U.S. Department of Labor Occupational Safety and Health Administration
  (OSHA) and/or the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as
  documented in 29 CFR § 1910 and 40 CFR § 311.

Hazardous substance exposure records may include, but are not limited
to, the following documents: employee exposure to hazardous substances
records; summaries of background data relevant to environmental
monitoring and measuring activities; and analyses using exposure or
medical records. They should be retained for the duration of employment
plus thirty years in accordance with 29 CFR § 1910.1020\[d\]\[1\]\[ii\]
and 40 CFR § 311.1.

Workplace safety training and risk reduction records may include, but
are not limited to, written certifications of hazard assessments,
written certifications documenting the provision of personal protective
equipment (PPE), and certifications that the employees understood the
PPE training. They should be retained for the duration of employment in
accordance with 29 CFR § 1910.132 and 40 CFR § 311.1.

Bloodborne pathogen immunization records concern employees' vaccination
against bloodborne pathogens, such as copies of vaccination status,
records and dates of vaccinations, written certifications of
vaccination, and vaccine declinations, among other records. Bloodborne
pathogens include, but are not limited to, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, and
human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). They should be retained for the
duration of employment plus thirty years in accordance with 29 CFR
1910.1020 \[d\]\[1\]\[i\] and 29 CFR 1910.1030 \[h\]\[1\]\[iv\].

- **Drug or Alcohol Testing Records.** These records document drug or

  Drug-Free Workplace Act (41 U.S. Code 81), Alabama's drug-free

  internal policies and procedures adopted by the agency. Records
  related to positive tests or refusals may include positive test
  results, refusals to be tested, follow-up test results, scheduling
  records for follow-up tests, and federally required reports and should
  be maintained for five years. Information obtained from previous
  employers concerning drug and alcohol test results of employees should
  be retained for three years, while negative and cancelled test results
  and all other records should be maintained for one year. All stated
  retentions comply with federal regulations found in 49 CFR § 40.333.

- **Employee Medical Records Not Otherwise Described.** These records
  include employee health documentation maintained by the agency in its
  capacity as an employer and not described elsewhere. Records may
  include, but are not limited to, pre-employment physicals, physician

  laboratory test results, or medical excuses for absenteeism or
  tardiness from a physician. Falling outside the scope of this series
  are records documenting agency compliance with the Family Medical
  Leave Act (FMLA), Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), drug or
  alcohol testing programs, and applicable Occupational Safety and
  Health Administration (OSHA) and/or Environmental Protection Agency
  (EPA) regulations. These records should be maintained in a separate
  file from the agency's employee personnel file. The three-year
  retention reflects the federal record keeping requirements of the Fair
  Labor Standards Act (FLSA), Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
  (EEOC), and the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA).

serves as the Commission's staff, in cooperation with representatives of
the Agency. The RDA lists records created and maintained by the Agency
in carrying out their mandated functions and activities. It identifies
records which must be maintained permanently and ultimately transferred
to the ADAH; establishes retention periods for temporary records; and
provides the legal authority for the Agency to implement destruction of
eligible records.

Alabama law requires public officials to create and maintain records
that document the business of their offices. These records must be
protected from "mutilation, loss, or destruction," so that they may be
transferred to an official's successor in office and may be made
available to members of the public. Records also must be kept in
accordance with auditing standards approved by the Examiners of Public
Accounts (Code of Alabama 1975 § 36-12-2, 36-12-4, and 41-5A-21). For
assistance in implementing this RDA, or for advice on records
disposition or other records management concerns, contact the agency
records manager or ADAH Archives Division at (334) 242-4452.

## Explanation of Records Requirements  {#explanation-of-records-requirements .H2}

The RDA shall govern the disposition of all records, regardless of
format, created by the agency from creation to dissolution. Please
contact the staff of the Department of Archives and History before
destroying any records created prior to 1940.

This RDA supersedes any previous records disposition schedules or RDAs
governing the retention of the records created by the Agency. Copies of
superseded schedules are no longer valid and may not be used for records
disposition.

The RDA establishes retention and disposition instructions for records
listed below, regardless of the medium on which those records may be
kept. Electronic mail, for example, is a communications tool that may
record permanent or temporary information. As for records in any other
format, the retention periods for e-mail records are governed by the
requirements of the subfunctions to which the records belong.

Certain other record-like materials are not actually regarded as
official records and may be disposed of under this RDA. Such materials
include (1) duplicate record copies that do not require official action,
so long as the creating office maintains the original record for the
period required; (2) catalogs, trade journals, and other publications
received that require no action and do not document agency activities;
(3) stocks of blank stationery, blank forms, or other surplus materials
that are not subject to audit and have become obsolete; (4) transitory
records, which are temporary records created for short-term, internal
purposes, may include, but are not limited to, telephone call-back
messages, drafts of ordinary documents not needed for their evidential
value, copies of material sent for information purposes but not needed
by the receiving office for future business, and internal communications
about social activities; (5) honorary materials, plaques, awards,
presentations, certificates, and gifts received or maintained by the
agency staff. They may be disposed of without documentation of
destruction.

## Requirement and Recommendations for Implementing the Records Disposition Authority (RDA) {#requirement-and-recommendations-for-implementing-the-records-disposition-authority-rda .H2}

**Requirement**

Under the Code of Alabama 1975 § 41-13-21, "no state officer or agency
head shall cause any state record to be destroyed or otherwise disposed
of without first obtaining approval of the State Records Commission."
This Records Disposition Authority constitutes authorization by the
State Records Commission for the disposition of the records of the
Agency as stipulated in this document.

One condition of this authorization is that the Agency submit an annual
Records Disposition Authority (RDA) Implementation Report on its
activities, including documentation of records destruction, to the State
Records Commission.

Unless otherwise stipulated in this document, the Agency must transmit
all permanent records which are no longer in active use to the ADAH.

**Recommendations**

In addition, the Agency should make every effort to establish and
maintain a quality record-keeping program by conducting the following
activities:

The Agency should designate a staff member in a managerial position as
its records liaison/records manager, who is responsible for: ensuring
the development of quality record keeping systems that meet the business
and legal needs of the agency, coordinating the transfer and destruction
of records, ensuring that permanent records held on alternative storage
media (such as microforms and digital imaging systems) are maintained in
compliance with national and state standards, and ensuring the regular
implementation of the agency's approved RDA.

Permanent records in the Agency's custody should be maintained under
proper intellectual control and in an environment that will ensure their
physical order and preservation. ADAH archivists are available to work
with Agency staff in determining the best location and storage
conditions for permanent records.

Destruction of temporary records, as authorized in this RDA, should
occur agency-wide on a regular basis -- for example, after the
successful completion of an audit, at the end of an administration, or
at the end of a fiscal year. Despite the RDA's provisions, no record
should be destroyed that is necessary to comply with requirements of the
Open Meetings Act, audit requirements, or any legal notice or subpoena.

The agency should maintain full documentation of any computerized
record-keeping system it employs. It should develop procedures for: (1)
backing up all permanent records held in electronic format; (2) storing
a back-up copy off-site; and (3) migrating all permanent records when
the system is upgraded or replaced. If the agency chooses to maintain
permanent records solely in electronic format, it is committed to
funding any system upgrades and migration strategies necessary to ensure
the records' permanent preservation and accessibility.

Electronic mail contains permanent, temporary, or transitory record
information. Although e-mail records can be printed out, filed, and
retained according to the RDA's requirements, the office should
preferably employ an electronic records management system capable of
sorting e-mail into folders and archiving messages having long-term
value.

The staff of the State Records Commission or the Examiners of Public
Accounts may examine the condition of the permanent records maintained
in the custody of the Agency and inspect records destruction
documentation. Agency records managers and/or the ADAH archivists are
available to instruct the staff in RDA implementation and otherwise
assist the Agency in implementing its records management program. For
more information, please call the ADAH Archives Division at
334-242-4452.

The State Records Commission adopted this records disposition authority
on October 16, 2024.
